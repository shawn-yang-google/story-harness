# Draft after Patch 10 (Round 5)

Marcus Vance watched the submit button snap into the exact center of the login box after forty-five minutes and three Stack Overflow tabs of pure, unadulterated bliss. 

Six months ago, his pulse would have been redlining at a hundred and ten while he screamed at three hundred senior developers about Kubernetes clusters. Now, he was a ghost—a mid-level code monkey at Synthetix with no direct reports, no board meetings, and only the beautiful, brain-dead simplicity of Jira tickets. 

Then, the overhead monitors strobed crimson. 

The open-plan office erupted into a chorus of swearing and frantic typing. Marcus kept his headphones on for a long moment, savoring the last dregs of his peace, before finally sliding them off. 

Greg, the perpetually sweating CTO, was already hyperventilating near the central command desk. "Kill the US-East cluster. Hard reset."

A junior engineer—pale, hands shaking over his keyboard—stared at his screen. "We'll drop three million sessions, Greg. The failover is looping."

"Do it before the primary melts."

The double doors swung open as Richard Sterling, a CEO seemingly born wearing a Patagonia vest, marched in with a tight jaw, trailed by a terrified intern pushing a rolling AV cart. On the monitor sat Evelyn Vance—no relation to Marcus, but the lead VC board member, dialed in from a pristine, white-walled office. 

"Greg," Richard said, his voice dangerously quiet. "My phone is ringing. Why is it ringing?"

"Cascade failure," Greg stammered, wiping his forehead. "I'm initiating a hard reset."

Evelyn's voice cut through the laptop speakers, dry as gin. "A hard reset. How bold. I'll let legal know to cancel their weekend plans. Wiping twelve hours of enterprise data should yield some fascinating lawsuits."

Richard flushed, a vein throbbing at his temple. "It's a necessary amputation, Evelyn." He glared at Greg. "Execute it."

Marcus watched Greg's trembling finger reach for the terminal. *Not my circus. Not my monkeys.* He had promised himself he was a drone now. But the sheer, staggering incompetence of running a monolithic database held together by duct tape and prayers—it was a physical itch. He looked at the junior dev, who was about to throw up into his wastebasket. 

Marcus stood up without announcing himself, walking over to step directly into Greg’s space and force the CTO to stumble back from the keyboard. 

"Hey," Greg yelped. 

"Security," Richard barked, stepping forward. "Get this idiot away from the console."

Marcus ignored them as his fingers flew across the keys, his muscle memory taking over to kill the redundant microservices and begin typing out a new routing protocol from scratch without bothering to explain the garbage collection traffic jam. 

"He's bypassing the load balancer," the pale junior engineer whispered, eyes wide.

When Marcus hit *Enter*, the crimson strobing stopped, plunging the room into the quiet hum of cooling fans as the error cascade froze and reversed on the main board. Green lights rippled across the dashboard as the database unlocked. 

The engineering floor let out a collective breath. 

Richard straightened his fleece, his face mottled purple. He pointed a shaking finger at Marcus. "Clear your desk. You're done. You don't touch production without authorization."

"Richard." 

Evelyn's voice from the cart was quiet. Dangerous. 

Richard froze. "Evelyn, I apologize for this breach in protocol—"

"Shut up and pivot the camera," she said. 

The intern hastily rotated the cart. Marcus found himself staring into the webcam. He crossed his arms and didn't flinch. 

Evelyn leaned closer to her screen, her eyes tracking the terminal logs visible on the monitor behind Marcus. "That bypass... You spun up a virtual shard using a recursive loop." She paused, her brow furrowing. "That's a proprietary NovaTech protocol."

"I wrote it," Marcus said flatly. "I can use it."

Evelyn stared. "Bring the camera closer." 

As the intern pushed the cart forward, Evelyn studied Marcus's face until a slow, sharp smile spread across her lips. "Marcus Vance. I'll be damned. I read you retired to a monastery after the IPO."

"I'm pacing myself," Marcus said. 

Richard looked frantically between the screen and Marcus's cheap lanyard. "Evelyn, what is going on? Who is this?"

"The man who built the infrastructure you've been trying—and failing—to copy for three years, Richard," Evelyn said, not taking her eyes off Marcus. "Why are you wearing a junior badge?"

"Because junior badges don't have to attend board meetings," Marcus said. "And nobody asks me to synergize paradigms."

Richard swallowed hard, the color draining from his face as he looked at the man he had tried to fire. "I... I wasn't informed..."

"Clearly," Evelyn interrupted. "Well, Marcus, your vacation is over. Richard, step away from the desk. Marcus, I want this entire backend gutted and rebuilt. You have carte blanche."

Marcus looked around the room. The junior devs were staring at him in stunned silence. They were brilliant kids suffocating under the weight of MBA buzzwords and terrible management. If he walked away, they'd be forced to build another monolithic death trap. 

He looked down at the keyboard, feeling the familiar, heavy mantle of responsibility settling back onto his shoulders. 

"I'm not taking a management role," Marcus warned. 

"I don't care what title you put in your email signature," Evelyn countered smoothly. "Fix my ship before Richard sinks it."

Marcus sighed, opening a new terminal to begin rebuilding the architecture. "I'm going to need a lot of server space. And we're killing the morning stand-ups."
