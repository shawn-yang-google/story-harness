# The Defining Dimension

No matter how many dimensions elaborate a role, they do not carry equal weight and focus. One tends to stand in the foreground, defining a character's essential spirit, while the others complete the portrait.

## The Principle
- A protagonist may house many more than three dimensions.
- First-circle supporting roles generally feature only one or two.
- Among all dimensions, one is so crucial to identity that if it were removed, the character would vanish with it.

## Identifying the Defining Dimension
- Ask: which contradiction is so essential that without it, the character ceases to exist?
- The other dimensions could inhabit someone else. The defining dimension belongs to this character alone.

## Case Study: Graham Greene
- Greene's nine dimensions: self-hating/self-glorifying, disciplined/self-destructive, romantic/cynical, devout Catholic/lifelong adulterer, Nobel-nominated novelist/pulp hack, strict theologian/moral relativist, salon communist/closet monarchist, anti-imperialist/postcolonial parasite, civilized/drugged to distraction.
- His defining dimension: **Artist/Hack**. The creative battle Greene waged within himself was his alone. Remove it and he vanishes.

## Case Study: [EPIC_HERO]
- Eight dimensions: Pragmatic/Idealistic, Obedient/Defiant, Truthful/Deceitful, Brilliant/Blundering, Protecting/Jeopardizing, Honorable/Thieving, Cool/Raging, Faithful/Unfaithful.
- His defining dimension: **Brilliant/Blundering**. He devised the Trojan Horse but blundered into Polyphemus's cave. His impulsive adventures risk his life, but his storytelling saves it.

## Application
- For every character, designate one dimension as the defining foreground contradiction.
- Test it by imagining the character without it. If the character survives the removal, it is not the defining dimension.
- A dimension is a **consistent** contradiction: not a one-time event but a recurring, variable, unpredictable, and unresolvable pattern.
