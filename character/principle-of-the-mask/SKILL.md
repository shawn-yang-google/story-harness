# Principle of the Mask

"Nothing is what it seems" applies directly and deeply to character creation. Characterization expresses only who a character appears to be, not who she is in fact.

### The Mask Principle

- Explicit and implicit traits form surfaces that hide a truth. Characterization is the mask; true character lives behind it.
- When characterization is credible and intriguing, the audience thinks: "Interesting, but who is she really? Honest or dishonest? Loving or cruel? Strong or weak?"
- A clichéd characterization is the same inside as outside — like a block of cement. No mask, no mystery, no drama.

### Nine Techniques for Expressing Characterization

1. **Simile**: Compare to other people or things ("He looked like a dishonest Abe Lincoln").
2. **Metaphor**: Directly link to figures from history or culture ("She's the [MOB_PROTAGONIST] of self-control").
3. **Correlations**: Extend through personal habits or possessions.
4. **Contrasts**: Compare generally ("She's unlike the typical college grad").
5. **Oppositions**: Contradict sharply ("She's an anti-intellectual PhD").
6. **Telling**: Direct description when needed ("She stood six feet, two inches tall").
7. **Showing**: Actions for eye or ear that activate imagination.
8. **Self-assessments**: What the character says about herself (may or may not be true).
9. **Other-assessments**: What one character says about another (subject to bias).

### Application

- Design the mask to be fascinating enough that the audience longs to see behind it.
- Use the gap between characterization and true character to power every revelation.
- The question "Who is she, really?" should remain alive until the climax delivers the answer through action under pressure.
