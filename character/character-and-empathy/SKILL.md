# Character and Empathy

Empathy with a character calls for refined sensitivity. Vicarious identification excites our senses and energizes our minds. Characters empower us to reflect, to know ourselves from within and without.

## The Competitive Principle
- Henry James said the only reason to write fiction is to compete with life.
- The only reason to create a character is to **compete with humanity**—to conjure someone more complex, more revealing, more magnetic than anyone we might meet.
- If stories and characters didn't compete with reality, we wouldn't write them.

## What We Want from Characters
- From a well-told story: to live in a world we could never experience.
- From a well-told character: to experience a life we would never live through a person we could never forget.

## The Mechanics of Empathy
- Characters show who and why we are who we are in all our strangeness, inconsistency, duplicity, and hidden beauty.
- Mirror neurons fire: vivid prose descriptions and dialogue ignite neurological empathy and give characters their heightened presence.
- Onstage and on-screen, actors bring the writer's creations to life. The audience deepens, refines, and seals each performance with personal perspectives.
- Every character acquires unique shadings while she works her way into our psyche.

## Characters as Homes in Minds
- Memorable characters find a home in our minds by drawing us into a shared humanity.
- A memorable character can be separated from her story and then held in the imagination, encouraging us to send our thoughts into spaces between scenes, into her past and future.
- Like images in dreams, well-written characters are more vivid than their real-life counterparts because, at heart, characters **symbolize the human spirit**.

## Application
- Design characters that create vicarious identification strong enough to compete with the reader's actual relationships.
- Ensure the character can be extracted from the plot and still resonate independently in the reader's imagination.
