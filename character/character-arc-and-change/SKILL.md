# Character Arc and Change

Change, when it occurs, arcs along a character's defining dimension. An age-old puzzle—the Ship of Theseus Paradox—asks whether gradual repair preserves identity or creates something new. Every author must decide: does damage change the core identity or not?

## Two Theories of Identity

### The Theory of Perpetual Identity
- A child at birth is a clean slate, but life inscribes unique patterns that create persistent identity.
- Shifts in characterization take place around a stable center. Once the pattern is set, choices make her more and more herself.
- [VICTORIAN_NOVELIST]: "Even while we rave on the heights, [we] behold the wide plain where our persistent self pauses and awaits us."
- Example: Alex in *[DYSTOPIAN_FILM]* both inflicts and suffers hideous pain but stays steadfastly the same.

### The Theory of the Changing Identity
- The self-aware human mind frets over its urges, charting the push and pull of instinct and reason, striving to become different.
- Change is not only possible but essential.
- Example: Lucifer in *Paradise Lost* arcs from Angel of Light (Good) to Satan, Angel of Darkness (Evil).

## Positive Arcs
- End on a positive charge. Often begin when a character senses a negative state and resists change until bitter truths overcome reluctance.
- [STRICT_NUN]: Arrogance → Humility (*Doubt*).
- [TIME_LOOP_PROTAGONIST]: Selfishness → Selflessness (*[TIME_LOOP_COMEDY]*).
- [DISGRACED_PROFESSOR]: Self-deception → Self-awareness (*Disgrace*).

## Negative Arcs
- Alternate positive/negative cycles deepening in impact until ending in tragedy.
- Often begin with youthful fantasy or naïveté. Reality punctuates dreams until an irreversible truth.
- [ASPIRING_SCHOLAR]: Fantasy → bitter Reality (*[TRAGIC_PROTAGONIST] the Obscure*).
- [FALLEN_HERO]: Good → Evil (*[SPACE_OPERA]*).
- Arcs seldom trace smooth lines—dynamic zigzags prevent repetition.

## Change from Three Points of View
- **Character's POV**: Does she see her past self as herself or someone else? Reactions range from clarity (Red in *Shawshank*) to confusion (Ursula in *The Rainbow*) to anger ([AGING_MAN] in *[AGING_MAN]'s Last Tape*).
- **Author's POV**: A healthy past strengthens identity; a traumatic past fragments it. Four possible effects of trauma: obsession, repression, fantasy, or balance.
- **Reader's POV**: Twin curiosities—who are these characters at heart, and who might they become—hold interest to the last moment.

## Application
- Choose a theory of identity before designing the arc.
- Arc change along the defining dimension, not a secondary trait.
- Move in zigzags, never in a monotone positive or negative line.
