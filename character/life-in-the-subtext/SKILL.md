# Life in the Subtext

Text is what the world sees — words and actions forming characterization. Subtext is what lives behind the eyes — conscious thoughts unexpressed and subconscious drives inexpressible.

### Text vs. Subtext

- **Minor characters** are text only. They act out exactly who they seem to be — deliberately denied depth and an inner life worth knowing.
- **Major characters** are not who they seem. Their text of characterization masks the mystery of the core self alive in the subtext, sparking the question: "Who is this character... really?"
- A beautifully conceived multidimensional character turns readers and audiences into psychics — they sense what the character is really thinking, feeling, and wanting.

### How Subtext Works

- The partition of text and subtext is essential to sanity. If the invasive world cannot be kept outside the mind, the inner life becomes unlivable.
- Characters themselves peer into each other's subtextural depths: Can I believe him? What does he really mean? Who does he care about?
- Introspective characters rerun personal experiences like TV episodes, searching through subtexts, analyzing actions, trying to figure out who they really are.

### The Writer's Challenge

- Depth can only be intuited — two people experiencing the same character often come away with very different interpretations.
- The subtextural self can never be fully explained in words. No one can tell you what to think about a character, not even the character himself in his most heartfelt confessions.
- Godlike omniscient narrators of the greatest novels imply more than they tell, leave far more unsaid than said.

### Application

- Design the gap between text and subtext as the primary engine of audience involvement.
- Let the audience discover hidden truths on their own — imply rather than explain.
- Of the two kinds of storytellers — those who explain and those who imply — prefer the latter.
