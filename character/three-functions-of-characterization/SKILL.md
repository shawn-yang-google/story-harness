# Three Functions of Characterization

Characterization supports your story with three primary functions. Every outer trait must serve at least one.

### 1. Credibility

- A writer's worst fear is disbelief. When readers think "I don't believe that kind of person would do that," they abandon the story.
- Credibility begins with honest connections between a character's mental, emotional, and physical traits and what they say, feel, and do.
- Honest characterizations induce surrender to even the most fantastic tellings — [YOUNG_WIZARD] and [SPACE_HERO] demonstrate this.

### 2. Originality

- We do not go to storytellers to learn what we already know. We pray for insights into life we've never had and characters we've never met.
- Originality begins with specificity. The more generalized the characterization, the more imitative and predictable the role.
- The more specific the characterization, the more original, surprising, and changeable the character.
- Exhaustive research into the world of your character yields one-of-a-kind characterizations no one has ever quite seen before.

### 3. Intrigue

- A unique characterization captures curiosity, making us want to know the true inner character behind the mask of outer traits.
- Clichéd characterization raises a question that answers itself: "Who is this character? Exactly who she seems to be."
- Like a block of cement, a clichéd character is the same inside as outside. To create intrigue, build fascinating worlds populated with equally fascinating originals.

### Application

- Test every characterization trait against all three functions: Does it build belief? Is it specific enough to be original? Does it make us curious about hidden depths?
