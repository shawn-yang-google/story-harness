# Six Types of Dimensions

Dimensions span between aspects of a role's outer characterization, between various inner selves, and between facets of the hidden self. These three levels often contradict one another, producing six distinct kinds of dimensions.

## 1. Contradiction Within Characterization
- Two observable traits clash: a woman spends an hour on makeup but doesn't brush her teeth; she abuses her partner but coddles her children.
- These fuse qualities of physical, personal, and social selves into intriguing behaviors.
- Characters are often aware of these traits but rationalize them as necessary.

## 2. Contradiction Between Characterization and True Character
- The outer mask hides an inner truth: an elderly woman dozes in a wheelchair, then awakens with a glint of ever-young romantic longing.
- Events under pressure strip away the surface to reveal what lives beneath.

## 3. Contradiction Between Characterization and Subconscious Desire
- A hyperactive woman who never stops doing things harbors a deeply calm hidden self that emerges only under threat.
- When a character says one thing and does another, she's either lying (knows the truth) or honest but undercut by a subconscious force she doesn't understand.

## 4. Contradiction Between Two Conscious Desires
- The adulterer's dilemma: devotion to her husband versus passion for her lover.
- A conscious, self-aware mind analyzes her inner contradiction, worries about it, struggles to make a choice.
- Playwrights like [ABSURDIST_PLAYWRIGHT], [RADICAL_PLAYWRIGHT], and Suzan-Lori Parks symbolize this by splitting the mind into a pair of bickering characters.

## 5. Contradiction Between Conscious and Subconscious Desire
- The lover's dilemma: fervent passion for a fiancée versus fear of commitment.
- *[CRIME_DRAMA_SERIES]* gives [CHEMIST_CRIMINAL] a secret second self, [CRIMINAL_ALIAS]—the doppelgänger that surfaces when the subconscious takes charge.
- Stevenson's *Jekyll and Hyde* and Dostoevsky's *The Double* cast this contradiction as dual characters with contradictory drives.

## 6. Contradiction Between Two Subconscious Motivations
- The family dilemma: the need to sacrifice desires for loved ones versus the need to sacrifice others for personal ambition.
- Perceived only through choices of action under pressure. [CLASSICAL_PLAYWRIGHT] projected contradictory subconscious feelings onto paired characters: noble father versus dastardly uncle in *[TRAGIC_PRINCE]*.
- *[PSYCHOLOGICAL_THRILLER]*: Nina's uptight White Swan versus her voluptuous, stifled [PSYCHOLOGICAL_THRILLER] war within her subconscious until they join forces in a glorious, life-consuming performance.
