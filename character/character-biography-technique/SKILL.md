# Character Biography Technique

Every character has a life history trailing back to birth. Compile extensive biography — not for the page, but for the foundational knowledge that sets off creative fireworks.

### Core Method

- Follow [CONTEMPORARY_NOVELIST]'s approach: devise thousands of biographical bits per protagonist. Few will reach the page, but they provide the knowledge that fuels creative choices.
- Look for **patterns** rather than singularities. Redundant emotional experiences deposit residues: repeated traumas cause PTSD; daily coddling produces egoists.
- Track how imprints left by recurring emotions affect motivations (seek/avoid), temperaments (calm/nervous), dispositions (optimistic/pessimistic), and personalities (charming/irritating).

### Focus on Early Adolescence

- In preteen days, a character first dreams about the future, testing possible meanings and purposes.
- A positive experience (inspirational teacher, personal epiphany) may commit him to a lifelong goal.
- A negative first glimpse produces confusion, doubt, fear, shame, anger, depression — he becomes a teenager.
- The identity crisis that follows determines who he becomes when a workable identity finally solidifies.

### Application

- Build biography backward from the inciting incident to birth. A character rarely abandons childhood traits in adult conflicts.
- He will often react to a person in the present as if they were someone from his past.
- Value the richness of the past over the void of the future — the remembered comes with textures and tensions; the imagined offers blanks to be filled.
- From the character's point of view, he can only hope the future will repeat a pattern he's coped with before.
