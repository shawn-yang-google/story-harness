# Setting as Characterization

A character's collisions with physical, social, and personal worlds etch characterization trait by trait. Nothing in a story's setting is neutral.

### Eight Setting-Character Relationships

1. **Extension**: Setting submerges a character in belongings until possessions become extensions of core self. ("Show me what a man owns and I'll tell you who he is." — Balzac)
2. **Antagonism**: Setting releases forces that block desires. Unexpected gaps between expectation and reality send quakes to the subconscious.
3. **Grand Metaphor**: Setting and cast form one metaphor for reality — they define each other like mirror opposites.
4. **Mental Flood**: Setting floods the mind with objects, people, and memories streaming through thoughts.
5. **Background**: Setting recedes as foreground overflows with character portraits.
6. **Indifference**: Setting exists so separately that characters seem isolated.
7. **Willful Objects**: Objects appear to have wills of their own.
8. **Objects as Characters**: Setting's objects transform into cast members.

### Moral Imagination

- An author with strong moral imagination sees meaning in every aspect of setting — nothing is neutral, not cop, prop, or raindrop.
- Marshal charged values so everything rings positively, negatively, or with ironic fusion of both.
- Without moral imagination, a setting amounts to little more than furniture.

### Application

- Ask two questions: (1) How does my setting affect my characters' personalities? (2) How do its antagonistic forces thwart my characters' desires?
- Design physical, social, and personal settings to maximize conflict and characterization depth.
