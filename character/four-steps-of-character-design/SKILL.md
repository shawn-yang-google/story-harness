# Four Steps of Character Design

The golden ideal: conceive a compelling, complex nature rich in human promise but incomplete. Then progressively realize her unfulfilled potential through pressure-filled decisions until the climax propels her to the absolute limits of her humanity. Nothing left undiscovered, nothing left unexpressed.

## Step 1: Preparation
**Principle: At the inciting incident, major characters are incomplete.**

- Characters have never experienced their mental, emotional, and moral possibilities to their absolute limits. They are incomplete but unaware of it.
- **Character need** is a void in a role's humanity: unthought intelligence, underfelt emotion, unused talent, underlived life.
- Before the story begins, take the character's measure: IQ, EQ, willpower, imagination, empathy, courage.
- Ask: What event would send my protagonist along a spine of action that ends in a climax of such power that she experiences her humanity at its supreme limit?
- That answer becomes the **inciting incident**, perfectly tuned to the protagonist's need.

## Step 2: Revelation
**Principle: A story's dynamic of events reveals true character in contrast with or contradiction to characterization.**

- The vast majority of genres reveal an inner truth but do not arc their protagonists.
- The most powerful revelations occur when events put a character at maximum risk.
- A choice between two deaths, or between losing and gaining things of equal value, strips away public personae and reveals the core self.
- Test with two questions: Who do we *think* she is at the beginning? Who do we *know* she is by the climax?

## Step 3: Change
**Principle: Character change puts dimension into action.**

- Change arcs along a character's defining dimension.
- **Positive arcs** end on a positive charge: often begin when a character senses a negative state, resists change, then overcomes reluctance.
- **Negative arcs** alternate positive/negative cycles that deepen in impact until ending in tragedy.
- Arcs seldom trace smooth lines—they move in dynamic zigzags. Repetition is the writer's mortal enemy.
- Every author needs a personal theory: does damage change the core identity, or does it stay permanent?

## Step 4: Completion
**Principle: The finest works fulfill their protagonist's need and desire at the limits of human experience.**

- **Desire**: a character's persistent purpose, her unreached goal.
- **Need**: an empty inner space, a potential that craves realization. Only the author sees it.
- At climax, final turning points put capacities under enormous pressure. The character experiences herself at the limit of her powers.
- She becomes a completed character—nothing left to reveal, nothing left to change.
- Characters are never aware of their need. Only the author knows what's latent and what the most complete state looks like.
