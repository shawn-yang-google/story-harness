# Narrator Design

The narrator is a character-level design decision. Choose voice (first, second, third person), medium (page, stage, screen), and reliability to shape how the audience accesses truth.

### Reliable Narrators

- **First-person** (onstage): Tells facts but emotional memories cloud recollections ([DREAMING_NARRATOR] in *[MODERN_PLAY]*).
- **First-person** (on page): Quiet, empathetic observers of adventurous characters (*[CLASSIC_NOVEL]*, *[CLASSIC_NOVEL]*).
- **Third-person**: Trustworthy, all-knowing voices (*[CONTEMPORARY_NOVEL]*, *[MODERN_PLAY]*).

### Unreliable Narrators

Unreliable narrators serve two purposes: enhanced credibility and greater curiosity.

- **Enhanced Credibility**: Flawless characters seem unreal. Imperfect narrators reflect human reality — people misperceive, rationalize, and lie. All statements beginning with "I" are, to some degree, self-serving.
- **Greater Curiosity**: When a narrator lies, the audience grows ever more interested in discovering what's true.

### Unreliable Variants by Medium

- **On-screen**: Faulty memories (*[SITCOM_SERIES]*), faulty knowledge (*[FILM_DRAMA]*), outright deceit (*[CRIME_THRILLER]*), multiple POVs (*[CLASSIC_FILM]*).
- **On page**: Unstable minds (Poe's "[GOTHIC_STORY]"), immaturity (*[COMING_OF_AGE_NOVEL]*), deliberate deception (*[DETECTIVE_NOVEL]*).
- **Onstage**: Contradictory memories (*[MODERN_PLAY]*), dementia distortion (*[MODERN_PLAY]*), autism (*[MODERN_NOVEL]*).

### Application

- Match narrator reliability to the story's relationship with truth. Reliability builds trust; unreliability builds tension.
- Remember: the most subjective media for storytelling are the screen and the page — both can warp point of view with just a slight shift.
