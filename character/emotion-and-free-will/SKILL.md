# Emotion and Free Will

Emotion is the side effect of change. When a value at stake shifts charge, the character experiences feeling. Free will, meanwhile, shapes how readers interpret every choice.

### Emotion as Value Change

- Positive value shift (poverty to wealth) → positive emotion. Negative shift (love to loss) → negative emotion.
- Six paired emotions give the writer a palette of twelve: Love/Hate, Amity/Anger, Joy/Grief, Surprise/Shock, Pleasure/Disgust, Courage/Fear.
- Emotion hits with intense suddenness and fades; a feeling comes slowly and lasts. Joy is emotion; happiness is feeling. Grief is emotion; sadness is feeling.

### Characterization Shapes Emotion

- The underlying emotion is only a starting point. The mood, tone, and texture from a distinctive personality shapes primal emotion into genuinely original performance.
- Joy in one character might be a high-stepping song and dance; in another, a smile around the edges of his eyes.

### The Free Will Tension

- Character choices seem free and unpredictable when seen from the inciting incident looking toward climax, but inevitable and fated when looking back from climax to inciting incident.
- Free will in one direction; retroactive determinism in the other. This dual perspective gives stories their power.

### Four Pathways to Decision

1. **Circumstantial**: Life pushes a character one way whether he likes it or not.
2. **Subconscious**: The hidden self makes choices the core self never realizes.
3. **Instinctive**: Quick consideration; one choice takes hold spontaneously.
4. **Rational**: Slow deliberation, pros and cons, judicious weighing — usually precedes a character's greatest mistakes.

### Application

- As author, all choices are ultimately yours. Design the illusion of freedom that becomes the appearance of fate.
