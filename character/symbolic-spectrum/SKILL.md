# Symbolic Spectrum

Every character means more than itself. All figures are metaphors for social identity (mother, boss) or inner identity (good, evil, wise) or both. The spectrum runs from brightest to dimmest.

### 1. Archetypes (Brightest)

- **Principle**: Archetypes turn essences into characters. Their symbolic power is so universal it can be endlessly varied yet never lose its basic pattern.
- Archetypes inhabit events (Sacred Birth, Fall from Grace), settings (Desert, Garden, Castle), objects (Light as Hope, Red as Passion), and roles (Star-Crossed Lovers, Outcast, Wizard).
- An archetypal character is the same inside as out — dimensionless, incapable of change, no subtext.
- The purer the archetype, the less we care about past or future, the more predictable the actions.
- **Key rule**: Veil symbolism behind never-seen-before characterization. If readers consciously recognize the symbol, the role flattens.

### 2. Allegories

- **Principle**: Allegories turn values into characters. Each embodies one facet — positive or negative — of a universal concept.
- Examples: Joy, Sadness, Anger in *[ANIMATED_FILM]*; Knowledge, Beauty, Death in *[ALLEGORICAL_PLAY]*; Democracy vs. Tyranny in *[ALLEGORICAL_NOVEL]*.

### 3. Types

- **Principle**: Types turn a behavior into a character. An animated adjective — Sleepy, Grumpy, Doc.
- Nondimensional supporting roles acting out a single trait of behavior or temperament.

### 4. Stock Characters (Dimmest)

- Functional roles defined entirely by profession or social position — the indifferent clerk, the talkative cabby.

### Application

- Know where each character falls on this spectrum. Complex protagonists vibrate with greater intensity when they strike an archetypal chord — but only if veiled beneath realistic characterization.
- For Speculative Fiction, the symbol weighs more than the person. For Realism, the person counts more than the symbol.
