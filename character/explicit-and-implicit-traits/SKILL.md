# Explicit and Implicit Traits

Complex characterizations display both genetically given and actively acquired traits. Every observable trait sits on a spectrum from positive to negative.

### Explicit Traits (Observable)

- **Names**: Names express more meaning in fiction than life. Avoid blatantly symbolic names unless seeking comedy.
- **Appearance**: Strip the character naked — age, build, skin, posture, walk. Then see what they choose to wear versus what they actually own.
- **Gender and Sex Life**: What does the character find irresistibly erotic? What is their gender identity?
- **Voice and Language**: Give each character a unique speech style — vocabulary, sentence structure, pitch, diction, imagery.
- **Gestures**: Facial expressions at 1/25th of a second. Mannerisms, tics, energy — hand waves, symbolic gestures, imitative gestures.
- **Profession and Recreation**: What they do for a living and for play. How much does it define who they are?
- **Home and Transportation**: The extended self puts its arms around all it owns.

### Implicit Traits (Inferable)

- **[CLASSIC_FILM]lity**: Five grand spectra — Openness/Closedness, Conscientiousness/Capriciousness, Extraversion/Introversion, Agreeableness/Argumentativeness, Rationality/Neuroticism.
- **Intelligence**: Consider IQ, EQ, and CIs (creative, intellectual curiosity, cultural intelligence). What are the character's habits of thought?
- **Attitudes and Beliefs**: Likes, dislikes, loves, hates, fears, dreads. Optimistic or pessimistic?
- **Emotion, Mood, Temperament**: Emotion is the side effect of change. Mood draws the same reaction from different experiences. Temperament is the childhood shadow that follows forever.

### The Three-Tenths Rule

- Like visual art, the optimal density is 1.3 on a 1-to-2 scale — express three-tenths of a character, leave seven-tenths unknown and mysterious. The audience fills in the rest from their own imagination.
