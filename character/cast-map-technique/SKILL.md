# Cast Map Technique

A reader's full psychological involvement comes not from the protagonist alone but from tensions between resemblances and differences among all cast members. Map these relationships visually.

### Building the Map

1. **Center Circle — Protagonist**: Place the most complex character at center. Give this role the majority of dimensions. Contradictions need performance time to reveal themselves.
2. **First Circle — Major Supporting Roles**: Distribute key supporting characters around the protagonist. Note their traits and dimensions. Counterpoint them with the protagonist so each evokes contrasting qualities in the others.
3. **Second Circle — Service Roles**: Limit these roles to distinctive traits only. They affect events but lack dimensions. Their actors round them out with intriguing personalities.
4. **Third Circle — Peripheral Roles**: Servants, passersby, clerks — functional parts that fill out the periphery.

### Design Principles

- If characters oppose on just one axis (good vs. evil), they trivialize each other and interest wanes.
- If opposed in complex ways, they arouse curiosities and empathies that draw energy from the storygoer.
- Each role must play a part in a creative strategy that enhances the storytelling — appearing in your imagination doesn't earn a place in your cast.
- As focus shifts character to character, the system shapes how they help and hinder each other, what they want, who they are.

### Application

- Create concentric circles for every story. Even short works benefit from mapping the counterpoint structure.
- Use the map to verify that every major character's dimensions are activated by at least one relationship.
- When incompatible desires fester into conflict, the links between characters break, transforming relationships — track these breaks on your map.
