# Conscious vs. Subconscious Desire

When a deeply conflicted protagonist begins pursuit of his conscious object of desire, his subconscious often stirs a counterdesire that contradicts his stated wants.

### Conscious Object of Desire

- When an inciting incident throws life out of balance, the protagonist conceives of something — a physical thing, a situation, or an experience — that would restore equilibrium.
- Depending on genre: a dead monster (Horror), a family reunion (Domestic Drama), a spiritual transformation (Evolution Plot).

### Subconscious Object of Desire

- The inciting incident also provokes a hidden hunger — a long dormant, unrecognized wish or grievance seeking its own satisfaction.
- This subconscious desire contradicts the conscious desire, making the character his own worst enemy.
- If conscious and subconscious desires are the same, they add neither complexity nor depth. The power comes from contradiction.

### Examples of Contradiction

- **Love vs. Hate**: [DRAMATIC_PROTAGONIST] in *[FILM_DRAMA]* — consciously seeks the perfect woman, subconsciously loathes every one he finds.
- **Fear vs. Courage**: Complex heroes like [HEROIC_CAPTAIN] pit conscious moral strength against a fear-soaked subconscious, making decisions while simultaneously gripped by terror.
- **Self vs. Other**: [STRESS_PARENT] in *[DIVORCE_DRAMA]* — begins as a self-absorbed workaholic, but abandonment awakens the suppressed need to be a loving human being.

### Application

- For subconscious desire to draw interest, it must directly contradict or sharply contrast with the character's conscious hopes.
- The exact balance of power between conscious and subconscious selves is up to the writer — neither dismiss it as instinct nor treat it mechanically.
