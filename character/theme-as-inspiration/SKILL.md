# Theme as Inspiration

All well-told stories express one essential idea: how and why life changes. But meaning must be discovered through writing, not dictated to the story.

### The Discovery Principle

- Writing is an exploration. Like a seafarer, an author sails into the story, never quite sure where he's headed. If nothing surprises him, he's steering a timeworn passage.
- As characters and events merge in climactic turning points, the writer discovers the story's meaning. The story surprises the writer with insight — not the reverse.
- Meaning arises from deep causes that arc a story's core value from negative to positive or positive to negative (hate to love, freedom to slavery, meaningless to meaningful life).

### The Danger of Working Backward

- When writers start with a belief and concoct plot and cast to demonstrate it, spontaneity hardens and the unforeseen is never discovered.
- Belief-fixated writers reduce stories to illustrated lectures and characters to mouthpieces.
- The practice is as old as medieval morality plays — [ALLEGORICAL_PLAY] surrounded by Virtue, Vice, Mercy, and Mischief.
- Today this tendency plagues Social Drama: starting with "Love cures addiction" yields plastic results that change no one's mind.

### Application

- Explore character creation first — let theme emerge from improvisation.
- Meaning discovered while exploring character is always more insightful than belief used to mechanize a plot.
- Search for hidden causes beneath the surface of society or within a character's subconscious mind.
