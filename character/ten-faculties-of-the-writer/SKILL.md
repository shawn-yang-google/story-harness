# Ten Faculties of the Writer

Characters need ten faculties from their creator. Each faculty equips the writer to bring depth, originality, and truth to character creation.

## 1. Taste
- Discriminate between bad and good in your own writing with guts and judgment.
- Cultivate an intrinsic disgust with banality and an eye for the vital versus the lifeless.
- Bad writing festers with sentimentality, narcissism, cruelty, self-indulgence, and lies.
- Sharp-eyed fiction expresses the gap between the fantasies that distract us and the realities they obscure.

## 2. Knowledge
- Acquire a godlike knowledge of the story's setting, history, and cast.
- Draw on constant observations of yourself and the humanity around you.
- Research the life sciences: psychology, sociology, anthropology, politics.
- When those don't teach enough, travel, discover, and explore firsthand.

## 3. Originality
- Creative originality calls for insight—seeing what's beneath, a hidden truth no one else has spotted.
- What's mistaken for originality is often recycling a forgotten influence.
- Genuine innovation is a **what**, not a **how**—a new thing, not a new way of doing an old thing.
- The avant-garde spirit today rips into content, not form, using story to expose lies the world has learned to live with.

## 4. Showmanship
- An author is first and foremost an entertainer.
- Combine a tightrope walker's daring with a magician's gift for concealment and surprising revelation.
- Give the reader the dual excitement of the true and the new.

## 5. Awareness of the Reader/Audience
- A reader's response calls on the same intelligence, logic, and emotional sensibility used in daily life.
- Fiction calls for long-term, uninterrupted concentration ending in meaningful, emotional satisfaction.
- Craft all characters with an eye to their moment-by-moment impact.

## 6. Mastery of Form
- Your original source of inspiration is the art form itself, not life.
- Story is a metaphor for life—a massive symbol expressing maximum meaning from minimal material.
- Form is the conduit for content, but ultimately they interlock: story is character, and character is story.

## 7. Hatred of Clichés
- A cliché was once so great that people recycled it for decades.
- Knowledge of your art form's history is a basic necessity.
- Spot clichés when you see them, and—more importantly—when you write them.

## 8. Moral Imagination
- "Moral" means all positive/negative binaries: life/death, love/hate, justice/injustice, hope/despair.
- "Imagination" means an author's full knowledge of time, place, and character powered by creative vision.
- A writer's values shape her unique vision of what is vital and what is trivial.

## 9. An Ideal Self
- When an author sits down to write, a transformation takes place.
- She becomes her most intelligent, most sensitive. Her talent, concentration, and honesty are at maximum pitch.
- This best possible self authors her truest insights into character.

## 10. Self-Knowledge
- "Everything I know about human nature I learned from me." —[MASTER_PLAYWRIGHT]
- We never know anyone the way we know ourselves. We spend our days essentially alone.
- All fine character creation begins and ends in self-knowledge.
- The more you penetrate the mysteries of your own humanity, the more you perceive the humanity in your characters.
