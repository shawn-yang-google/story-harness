# Four Levels of Antagonism

Levels and qualities of conflict range from physical to inner. Each level adds dimensions to a character. A character becomes only as complex as the antagonistic forces arrayed against him.

### Level 1: Physical Conflicts

- Natural environments, man-made systems, bodily maladies, and time's brevity.
- Nature is random as a tornado, fierce as a wolf pack, and morally indifferent as evolution.
- The body inflicts aggravations from illness to aging to an unfortunately shaped nose.

### Level 2: Social Conflicts

- Massive institutions harden into hierarchies where people feel little personal responsibility.
- Institutions shape people into roles, organize them in pyramids — no power at the bottom, great power at top, and jostling in between.
- Institutions deindividualize: people do things they normally wouldn't. Sociopaths constitute 1% of the population but 10% of Wall Street.

### Level 3: [CLASSIC_FILM]l Conflicts

- Public relationships value consequences; personal relationships value intentions.
- Intimacy creates conflicts like none other — friends embrace until betrayal, a mother's love shines until disrespect, romance explodes into optimism or crumples into pessimism.
- Feelings run deepest at this level. Dramas of personal catastrophe have greater impact than tales of social conflict.

### Level 4: Inner Conflicts

- All outer dimensions trace roots back to the mind. Complex characters pull storytelling inside until inner conflicts become more important than outer struggles.
- Conscious conflicts: thought-driven contradictions vying in the mind (Intelligent/Unintelligent, Impulsive/Reflective).
- Subconscious conflicts: wordless wars (Self vs. Other, Illusion vs. Delusion) — ineffable but glimpsed by the audience.

### Application

- For each trait, imagine its opposite. When one consistently contradicts another, the tension fuses into a dimension.
- Layer multiple levels to create protagonists capable of carrying full-length tellings.
