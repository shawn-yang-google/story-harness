# Misshapen Lines

Even a perfectly designed scene can be ruined by poorly constructed individual lines. Misshapen lines break the rhythmic "spell" and frustrate the reader's (or actor's) ability to find the core meaning.

## 1. Blurred Meaning
This occurs when a writer uses generic nouns and verbs instead of specific ones, leaving the character's intent fuzzy.

### Actionable Principles:
- **The Specificity Test:** Replace "He went over there" with "He limped to the vault."
- **Avoid Vague Pronouns:** Ensure the "it" or "they" in a sentence is crystal clear in context.

## 2. Mistimed Meaning
This is a failure of suspense or interest within the sentence itself.
- **Too Late:** The audience figures out the point of the sentence halfway through, and the rest is just "padding."
- **Too Soon:** The core information is dumped at the start, and the sentence trails off into irrelevance.

## 3. Mistimed Cues
In performance, the "core word" of a line should ideally be at the very end. This allows the other character to react immediately. 
- **The Flaw:** If the core word is in the middle, the actor/reader must wait for the "tail" of the sentence to finish before they can move to the next beat.

## Key Example ([MODERN_PLAY]):
- **Improper:** "I'm going to kill you if you touch that child again!" (The "kill" is too early; the "again" is the tail).
- **Proper:** "If you touch that child again, I'll kill you." (The "kill" is the cue; the reaction happens NOW).

## Practical Guidance:
Read your dialogue with an eye for the "impact word." Move that word to the end of the sentence whenever possible. Strip away the "tails" of sentences that add no new meaning.
