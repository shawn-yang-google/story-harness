# Conflict Levels

Conflict levels dictate the necessity and nature of dialogue. Understanding where your scene sits on the spectrum from physical to private allows you to calibrate speech effectively.

## The Four Levels of Conflict

1.  **Physical Conflict:** Man against nature, machine, or time. When survival is at stake, dialogue is minimal. Characters don't give speeches while running from a predator; they grunt, shout warnings, or remain silent to stay hidden.
2.  **Social Conflict:** Man against institutions, traditions, or society. Dialogue here is often formal, performative, or defensive. It deals with roles and masks.
3.  **[CLASSIC_FILM]l Conflict:** Man against man (intimate). This is the fertile ground for deep dialogue. It involves friends, lovers, and enemies who know each other's vulnerabilities.
4.  **Private Conflict:** Man against himself. This often manifests as internal dialogue or externalized self-revelation.

## Principles of Calibrated Dialogue

*   **The Inverse Ratio:** As conflict moves from the physical to the personal, the volume of dialogue typically increases. Physicality demands action; intimacy demands expression.
*   **Multilayered Conflict:** Complex stories embrace multiple levels simultaneously. A scene might feature social pressure (a wedding) overlaying deep personal resentment.

## Practical Guidance

*   **Calibrate Volume:** If your scene feels too "talky," check if the conflict level is too physical for the amount of speech.
*   **Identify the Core:** Ask yourself: Is this character fighting the world (Social), their partner ([CLASSIC_FILM]l), or their own shadow (Private)? Let the dialogue reflect that focus.
*   **Breaking Levels:** Use dialogue to shift conflict levels. A character can turn a physical standoff into a personal interrogation with a single well-placed question.
