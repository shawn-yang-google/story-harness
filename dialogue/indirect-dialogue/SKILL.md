# Indirect Dialogue

Indirect dialogue occurs when a character (or the narrator) paraphrases or summarizes a conversation rather than presenting it as a dramatized, line-by-line scene. It is a powerful tool for pacing and creating a specific narrative effect.

## The Power of Paraphrase
Sometimes, the *fact* of a conversation is more important than the specific words. Indirect dialogue allows the writer to skip over the mundane "hello-how-are-you" and get straight to the impact. It also allows the reader to imagine the scene, which can often be more vivid than a scripted exchange.

## Shifting to Farce or Tension
By repeating a reporting verb, a writer can create a sense of monotony, absurdity, or rising tension. If a character "suggested" five different things in a row, the reader feels the weight of their persistence without having to endure five separate scenes.

## Actionable Principles
- **Summarize the Routine:** Use indirect dialogue for conversations the reader has heard before or that don't require immediate conflict.
- **Invite Imagination:** When a character describes a "heated argument," the reader's mind will provide more personalized and believable insults than the writer might.
- **Control the Pace:** Use indirect dialogue to move quickly through days or weeks of interaction to reach the next "big" dramatized scene.

## Practical Guidance
Don't dramatize everything. If a conversation doesn't contain a pivotal action or a major revelation, see if it can be handled through indirect dialogue. This keeps the narrative drive high and reserves the "onstage" time for the most impactful moments.
