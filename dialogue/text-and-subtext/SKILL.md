# Text and Subtext

Text is the surface of a work — the words characters actually speak. Subtext is the inner substance — the meanings and feelings that flow below the surface.

## The Double Layer
- **Text:** "Hi, how ya doing? Oh look, you've lost weight!"
- **Subtext:** Depending on relationship and history, Character A could be *encouraging*, *seducing*, *taunting*, or *insulting* Character B

The same surface words can carry radically different subtextual actions. The possible subtextual actions under the most neutral expressions are as varied as the beings who take them.

## The Hitler Exception
Those rare people who put their unsaid directly into their said seem mechanical, unreal, inhuman. Hitler had no subtext — *Mein Kampf* was not a metaphor but a timetable for the holocaust. Because his visions were too horrible to believe, allied politicians spent the 1930s searching for consolation in a nonexistent subtext.

## Skillful Dialogue Creates Transparency
Text conceals a character's inner life from other characters while simultaneously allowing the reader/audience to see through the surface. This delivers the sensation of insight — the sense of reading a character's mind.

## Paralanguage Affects Subtext
Gesture, facial expression, tone of voice, dress, and movement influence how text is received. A character's experience of inner self and outer life is expressed with or without words.

## Practical Guidance
- Every line should have a gap between text and subtext — what's said vs. what's meant
- Test: if you remove the subtext and the line still works the same way, it's on-the-nose
- Ask: what is each character doing TO the other with these words?
- The reader/audience should feel like mind readers — understanding more than the characters themselves
