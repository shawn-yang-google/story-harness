# Word Choice Preferences

Great dialogue is built on a foundation of specific, effective word choices. While character voice allows for exceptions, these eight preferences serve as the "standard model" for compelling speech.

## The Eight Preferences:
1. **Concrete > Abstract:** "A Glock 19" is better than "a weapon."
2. **Familiar > Exotic:** Use the word that fits the character's world, not the one that shows off your vocabulary.
3. **Short > Long:** Shorter words often carry more emotional weight.
4. **Direct > Circumlocution:** Avoid talking *around* the point unless the character is intentionally stalling.
5. **Active > Passive:** "He hit me" vs. "I was hit by him."
6. **Short Speeches > Long:** Life is action/reaction. Long monologues are rare and must be earned.
7. **Expressive > Mimicry:** Capture the *flavor* of an accent or dialect, don't try to phonetically reproduce it.
8. **Eliminate Clutter:** Remove "filler" words (um, uh, like) unless they serve a specific character beat.

## The Emotional Scale
English has a "double vocabulary": Germanic (short, punchy, emotional) and French/Latin (longer, intellectual, distant).
- **High Emotion:** Use short, Germanic words. "I hate you."
- **Low Emotion/Intellectual:** Use longer, Latinate words. "I find your behavior reprehensible."

## The Exception: The [ELOQUENT_CRIMINAL] Effect
Sometimes, a character's "brand" is using exotic, long, and circumlocutory language (e.g., [ELOQUENT_CRIMINAL] in *[CRIME_DRAMA_SERIES]*). This only works because it is a deliberate, character-defining choice that contrasts with everyone else.

## Practical Guidance:
When editing, apply these preferences as a filter. If a line feels weak, try making it more concrete, shorter, and more active. Only deviate from these rules when you have a specific character reason to do so.
