# The Unsayable

The innermost sphere: subconscious drives and needs that incite a character's choices and actions. These energies cannot be expressed in words — even to the self — because they are mute and beyond awareness.

## The Principle
A character's truest nature can only be expressed when, under pressure, he chooses to act in pursuit of a life-defining desire. As antagonistic pressures build, choices reveal the hidden self, until a final choice under maximum pressure exposes the primal, irreducible self.

## Words Are Deeds
Language cannot express who a character actually IS — only who he seems to be. The Bible teaches a person is known by his deeds, not words. But the truth comes full circle: **words are deeds**. When a character speaks, he is doing something — comforting, bribing, begging, defying, remembering.

## Dialogue as Transparency
Fine dialogue creates a transparency that allows the reader/audience to gaze through text to the unsayable truth. The story-goer becomes a mind reader, often understanding the character better than the character understands herself.

## Key Insight
If everything could be expressed in words, we should stop telling stories and write essays. But at the rock bottom of being, unsayable energies in the subconscious demand expression — and dialogue is the vehicle that unites all three spheres (said, unsaid, unsayable).

## Practical Guidance
- The unsayable is revealed through **choices under pressure**, not through explanation
- Never have a character articulate their deepest drives — show those drives through action
- Dialogue wields double power: expressing characterization (the effable) while illuminating true character (the ineffable)
- When writing climactic scenes, ask: what subconscious need is driving this character's words?
