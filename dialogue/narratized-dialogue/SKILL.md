# Narratized Dialogue

Narratized dialogue occurs when a character steps out of the immediate scene to speak directly to the audience, the reader, or their own internal consciousness. It collapses the distance between the character's mind and the consumer of the story.

## The Vanishing Fourth Wall
Unlike dramatized dialogue, which is contained within the scene, narratized dialogue acknowledges the medium. In prose, first-person narration is essentially a continuous stream of narratized dialogue directed at the reader.

## Shifting the Addressee
The language a character uses changes radically depending on who they think is listening. 
- **Self-to-Self:** Fragmented, raw, and often coded in personal shorthand.
- **To Reader/Audience:** Explanatory, performative, and often seeking sympathy or understanding.
- **Dramatized:** Tactical and guarded.

## The Doctor Glas Comparison
Consider the three modes of expressing the same content:
1. **Self-to-Self:** "Why did I do it? The pills... his face. God."
2. **To Reader:** "You must understand, Dr. Glas was a man of logic, but that night, logic failed him."
3. **Dramatized:** "I gave him the medicine he required. Nothing more."

## Actionable Principles
- **Define the Target:** Always know exactly who the character is addressing in their narration.
- **Vary the Syntax:** Use narratized dialogue to reveal the character's "true" voice, which may be more complex or lyrical than their spoken dialogue.
- **Use as Counterpoint:** Narratized dialogue can provide a "truth" that contradicts the dramatized actions on the screen or page.

## Key Examples
- **Voice-Over:** A detective explaining the case while we see him drinking alone.
- **First-Person Prose:** The entire narrative of *The [COMING_OF_AGE_NOVEL]* is narratized dialogue.

## Practical Guidance
Use narratized dialogue to provide depth and subtext that cannot be captured in a scene. However, be careful not to let it become a crutch for "telling" what should be "shown."
