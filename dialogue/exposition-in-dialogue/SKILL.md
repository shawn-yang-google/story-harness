# Exposition in Dialogue

Exposition consists of the fictional facts—history, setting, and character details—that the reader needs to understand the story. The goal is to weave this information into dialogue so naturally that it becomes invisible.

## The Art of Parsing
The biggest mistake in exposition is the "info-dump." To make exposition effective, you must parse it out detail by detail. Give the reader only what they need to know at the exact moment they need to know it—and not a second before.

## Ranking Facts
Not all facts are created equal. 
1. **Critical Facts:** Need repetition or strong emphasis to ensure the audience remembers them.
2. **Atmospheric Facts:** Can be mentioned once as a single hint to build the world.
3. **Background Facts:** Only necessary if they drive a character's current motivation.

## Actionable Principles
- **Weaponize the Fact:** Never have characters tell each other things they both already know ("As you know, Bob, we've been brothers for thirty years"). Instead, have them use the fact as ammunition in an argument.
- **Wait for the Question:** Don't explain the magic system until a character is faced with a problem that only the magic system can solve (or cause).
- **Hide it in Emotion:** Weave facts into lines of high emotional intensity. A character screaming about a past betrayal is providing exposition, but the audience is focused on the anger.

## Key Examples
- **Bad Exposition:** Two scientists explaining basic physics to each other in a lab.
- **Good Exposition:** A pilot screaming at his co-pilot, "I told you that engine wouldn't hold after what happened in Dubai!" (We now know about a past failure and a current danger).

## Practical Guidance
If you find yourself writing long paragraphs of "talk" that explain the plot, stop. Find a way to turn that information into a secret, a lie, or a threat. Exposition should be a side effect of characters trying to get what they want.
