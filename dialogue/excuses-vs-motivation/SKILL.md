# Excuses vs. Motivation

To write authentic dialogue, one must distinguish between why a character *thinks* they are acting (the excuse) and the primal, subconscious need actually driving them (the motivation).

## 1. The Nature of Motivation
Motivations are deep-seated, often subconscious needs—for power, love, safety, or vengeance—that drive behavior across the entire story.

### Actionable Principles:
- **Identify the Primal Need:** Before writing, determine the character's "Want" and "Need." The "Need" is the true motivation.
- **Motivation is Non-Verbal:** Characters rarely state their true motivations directly. They act on them.

## 2. The Role of the Excuse
Excuses are conscious inventions characters use to rationalize their behavior to themselves and others. They are masks for deeper drives.

### Actionable Principles:
- **Write the Mask:** Let the character believe their own excuse. If a king invades a neighbor "to bring stability," let him speak passionately about peace while his actions scream "conquest."
- **The Gap is Character:** The distance between a character's excuse and their motivation defines their complexity. 

## Key Example: Richard III
- **The Excuse:** Richard claims his physical deformity prevents him from being a lover, so he "must" become a villain.
- **The Motivation:** Primal power-lust. He later proves he can be a brilliant seducer when it serves his path to the throne. His deformity is his shield, not his cause.

## Practical Guidance:
When a character explains "why I did it," treat that speech as a potential lie or a self-delusion. Look at their actions. If the actions contradict the speech, the speech is the excuse, and the contradiction is where the true character lives. Never let a character's self-explanation be the final word on their soul.
