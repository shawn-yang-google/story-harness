# Duelogue and Trialogue

Standard two-person "duelogues" often become flat and on-the-nose because the conflict is too direct. The "Trialogue" adds a third element that forces the conflict to become indirect and resonant.

## 1. The Duelogue Trap
A duelogue is face-to-face conflict. It often has the "resonance of a brick"—it's blunt, obvious, and leaves no room for subtext. 

## 2. [KAFKAESQUE_NOVEL]ogue Solution
A trialogue isn't necessarily three people; it's two people in conflict plus a "Third Thing" through which they funnel their struggle.

### Examples of the "Third Thing":
- **An Object:** The canaries in *[CRIME_NOVEL]* or the blue crystal meth in *[CRIME_DRAMA_SERIES]*.
- **An Absent Person:** [ABSENT_ENTITY] in *[ABSURDIST_PLAY]*.
- **An Abstract Concept:** "The Culture" in *[MAGICAL_REALISM_NOVEL]* or "The Future" in a political debate.

## 3. Why it Works
When characters fight *over* a third thing rather than at each other, the dialogue automatically gains subtext. Instead of saying "I hate your parenting," they argue about the child's grades or the cleanliness of the kitchen.

## Key Example:
- **Duelogue:** "You don't love me anymore." "Yes I do." (Flat).
- **Trialogue:** They argue about a dying houseplant. One insists it needs more water; the other says it's already rotten at the roots. They are talking about their marriage, but the plant provides the "mask."

## Practical Guidance:
If a two-character scene feels stagnant, introduce an object, a task, or a third party (present or absent). Force the characters to interact with that third element while they try to get what they want from each other.
