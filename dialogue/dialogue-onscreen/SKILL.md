# Dialogue Onscreen

Film and television are visual media. In this context, dialogue often serves as a counterpoint to the image, and the camera’s ability to "see through" falsity demands a high degree of naturalism.

## The Camera's Magnification
The camera acts as a microscope on human behavior. It captures the slightest flicker of an eye or a subtle crack in the voice. Consequently, dialogue that feels "theatrical" or "staged" often rings false onscreen. Silence is frequently more powerful than speech.

## Screen-Specific Techniques
- **Voice-Over (VO):** Providing interiority without the character speaking in the scene. Often used for noir-style narration or internal reflection.
- **Direct Address (Breaking the Fourth Wall):** A character looking into the lens to speak to the viewer. This creates an intimate, often complicit relationship.
- **Counterpoint:** When the dialogue says one thing while the visuals show another (e.g., a character describing a "peaceful" day while we see a city in chaos).

## The Naturalism Imperative
Because the medium feels "real," characters should speak with the rhythms of actual life—overlapping speech, half-finished thoughts, and subtextual redirections.

## Actionable Principles
- **Less is More:** If an image can convey the emotion, cut the line.
- **Trust the Subtext:** Don't let characters say exactly what they feel. The camera will catch the truth in their eyes.
- **Use the VO Sparingly:** Ensure voice-over adds a new layer of meaning rather than just describing what we are already seeing.

## Practical Guidance
Write for the visual. Imagine the scene without sound—if it still works, the dialogue you add will be an enhancement, not a crutch.
