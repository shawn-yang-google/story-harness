# TV Dialogue Form

Television sits between the symbolic voice of the stage and the literal image of the cinema. The small screen (traditionally) favors the human face and the intimacy of conversation.

## Actionable Principles

*   **Balance of Voice and Image:** TV requires more dialogue than film but less than theatre. It relies on the "talking head" or the close-up, making conversation the primary driver of drama.
*   **[CLASSIC_FILM]l Relationship Focus:** TV thrives on domestic and workplace dynamics. Dialogue should focus on the ongoing friction between characters in a confined "family" unit.
*   **Economic Necessity:** Smaller budgets often force drama into interiors. When you can't show a battle, you have two characters discuss the strategy or the aftermath.

## Principles of TV Dialogue

*   **Intimacy:** Write for the "living room." Dialogue should feel like it's happening just a few feet away from the viewer.
*   **The Merging Future:** As screens grow and production values increase, TV dialogue is trending toward cinematic naturalism, but the core remains the face-to-face exchange.

## Key Examples

*   **[WHITE_HOUSE_DRAMA]:** Famous for "walk and talk" sequences. It uses high-speed, witty dialogue to create a sense of urgency and intelligence, compensating for the static nature of office environments.
*   **[CORPORATE_DRAMA]:** Balances corporate jargon with visceral, profane personal insults. The dialogue is the primary weapon in a low-budget (environmentally) power struggle.

## Practical Guidance

*   **Maximize the Close-Up:** Write lines that trigger emotional reactions visible on a character's face.
*   **Leverage Seriality:** Use the fact that viewers know these characters over dozens of hours. Lean into inside jokes, shared shorthand, and long-simmering resentments.
*   **Talk Through the Action:** If a scene feels slow, add a layer of dialogue that exposes character relationships while they perform a task.
