# Film Dialogue Form

In cinema, the camera acts as a microscope. It magnifies falsity, demanding a level of naturalism that theatre can ignore. Film is an art of image first, and dialogue must respect the visual's dominance.

## Actionable Principles

*   **The Naturalism Imperative:** Most film dialogue should sound like "found" speech—overheard, messy, and grounded in the physical reality of the scene.
*   **Write for the Actor:** Give the actor "subtextual room." Don't put everything into words; leave space for the look, the gesture, and the reaction.
*   **Beware of Improvisation:** While improvisation can create naturalism, it often dilutes dramatic structure. Scripted naturalism is a constructed illusion.

## The Three Exceptions to Naturalism

Heightened or stylized language works in film ONLY under these conditions:

1.  **Stylized Realism:** Exotic or heightened settings (e.g., 18th-century France, a futuristic dystopia, or the high-stakes world of Wall Street).
2.  **Nonrealism:** Fantasy, horror, or sci-fi genres where the world's rules are fundamentally different.
3.  **Extreme Characters:** Larger-than-life figures whose wit or personality justifies their verbal dexterity.

## Key Examples

*   **The Big Sleep (Marlowe/Vivian):** The famous racetrack metaphor dialogue. It is highly stylized and witty, but works because both characters are "extreme" and the setting is a noir fantasy of Los Angeles.
*   **Naturalistic Masterpieces:** Think of the works of Cassavetes or the Dardenne brothers, where dialogue is functional, overlapping, and seemingly unscripted.

## Practical Guidance

*   **Cut the Talk:** If a look can say it, delete the line.
*   **Ground the Speech:** Anchor dialogue in activity. Characters should be doing something physical (eating, fixing a car, walking) while they speak to distract from the artifice.
*   **Check for "Stagey-ness":** If a character delivers a three-minute monologue in a contemporary kitchen, it will likely feel "stagey." Break it up with visual reactions and environmental interruptions.
