# Write in Character

To write dialogue that rings true, you must become your characters. The writer is the first actor — slipping into each role to see the world from their subjective point of view.

## Two Angles of Approach

### Outside-In
Start with observable traits and work inward:
- Research the character's world, profession, culture, era
- Observe real people who share your character's circumstances
- Build characterization from external details — speech patterns, vocabulary, mannerisms
- Useful for establishing the character's social mask

### Inside-Out (The Magic If)
Start with the character's inner life and work outward:
- Ask: "If I were this character, in these circumstances, what would I say?"
- Slip your imagination into the character's mind
- See the world from their subjective point of view
- Feel their desires, fears, and pressures
- Let the words emerge from that emotional truth

The inside-out method is more difficult but more powerful — it produces dialogue that feels inevitable rather than constructed.

Before your characters speak, you must first inhabit them. Your performance on the page precedes any actor's performance on stage or screen.

## Practical Guidance
- For each major character, spend time "being" them before you write their dialogue
- Ask yourself: "If I were this person, with this history, in this situation, under this pressure — what would I say?"
- The answer that feels most honest — not most clever — is usually the right one
- When dialogue feels false, the problem is usually insufficient inhabitation of the character, not word choice
