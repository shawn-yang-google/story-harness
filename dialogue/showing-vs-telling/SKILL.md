# Showing vs. Telling in Dialogue

"Show, don't tell" is the most famous rule in writing, but it is often misunderstood. In dialogue, it means presenting scenes where characters struggle toward their desires, rather than forcing them to halt and explain themselves.

## The Telling Trap
"Telling" happens when characters become mouthpieces for the author. They stop acting and start explaining their history, their feelings, or the plot. This erases subtext and kills the momentum of the scene.

## The Showing Solution
"Showing" in dialogue means providing the *result* of an emotion or a fact. Instead of a character saying "I am sad because my father died," they might snap at a friend for making a harmless joke about parents. The audience "sees" the sadness through the behavior.

## The Dry-Cleaning Example
- **Bad (Telling):** "Harry, you know I've always been responsible, which is why I'm so upset that you forgot to pick up my dry-cleaning for the interview tomorrow."
- **Good (Showing):** "Where is it? Harry, the interview is at eight. Tell me you didn't leave it on the rack."

## Actionable Principles
- **Diagnose the "Why":** If a character is explaining why they are angry, they are telling. If they are throwing a plate, they are showing.
- **Check for Subtext:** Is there a gap between what is said and what is meant? If the dialogue is 100% literal, it is likely "telling."
- **Focus on Desires:** Characters should talk about what they want *now*, not what happened *then*.

## Key Examples
- **Telling:** "I feel so lonely in this big house since you left."
- **Showing:** "I've started leaving the television on in the other room. Just for the noise."

## Practical Guidance
Look for "feeling words" (sad, happy, angry) in your dialogue. Try to replace them with actions, tactical maneuvers, or specific, concrete details that evoke those feelings in the reader without naming them.
