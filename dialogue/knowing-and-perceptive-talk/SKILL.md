# Knowing and Perceptive Talk

The "Author's Whisper" is a common flaw where the writer's research, thematic intentions, or psychological insights are injected directly into a character's awareness, making them seem preternaturally wise or suspiciously well-informed.

## 1. Overly Knowing Talk
This occurs when a character possesses information or technical expertise they haven't earned, or when they speak with the author's bird's-eye view of the plot.

### Actionable Principles:
- **Resist the Research Dump:** You may have filled notebooks on 14th-century loom repair, but your character should only mention it if it serves a direct, immediate need.
- **Limit Character Awareness:** Keep characters trapped in their own limited perspectives. They shouldn't see the "thematic significance" of their actions.
- **Stay Behind the Line:** Ensure a clear separation between what *you* know as the creator and what *they* know as the created.

## 2. Overly Perceptive Talk
This is "[PSYCHOLOGIST]-level self-insight." Real people rarely understand their own deepest motivations, yet "perceptive" characters explain their traumas and drives with clinical precision.

### Actionable Principles:
- **Embrace Blind Spots:** Characters are most interesting when they are wrong about themselves. 
- **Subconscious vs. Conscious:** What a character thinks they are doing should differ from what they are actually doing.
- **Insight as Revelation:** If a character achieves self-insight, it should be the hard-won climax of an arc, not a casual observation in Scene 2.

## Key Example:
- **Knowing:** A peasant in a fantasy novel explaining the exact socio-economic factors leading to the fall of the empire.
- **Perceptive:** "I suppose I'm only pushing you away because I'm terrified of the intimacy my father denied me." (Real people usually just pick a fight over the dishes).

## Practical Guidance:
Audit your dialogue for "authorial intrusion." If a line sounds like it came from a textbook or a therapist's couch rather than a person in a struggle, rewrite it to be messier, more subjective, and less "correct."
