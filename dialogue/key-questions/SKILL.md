# Key Questions

Before writing dialogue, systematically answer questions at four levels. Each level builds on the previous, ensuring that every spoken word connects to character desire and story design.

## Story-Level Questions
- What does the protagonist want? (Object of desire)
- What is the super-intention? (The emotional hunger driving the quest)
- What forces of antagonism oppose the protagonist? (Physical, social, personal, inner)
- What is the spine of action? (The persistent pursuit from inciting incident to climax)

## Scene-Level Questions
- What is each character's scene intention? (What do they want RIGHT NOW?)
- What happens if they get it? (The scene would stop)
- What forces block them in THIS scene?
- What is the turning point? (The moment the value at stake changes charge)
- What value is at stake? (Love/hate, truth/lie, hope/despair, etc.)

## Beat-Level Questions
- What action does each character take in this beat? (Label with a gerund)
- What reaction does that action provoke?
- Does this beat TOP the previous beat? (If not, the scene isn't progressing)
- Is this beat a repetition of an earlier beat in different words? (If so, cut one)

## Dialogue-Level Questions
- What specific words would THIS character use to carry out THIS action?
- Does the vocabulary reflect the character's knowledge and personality?
- Is there subtext beneath the text? (If text = subtext, it's on-the-nose)
- Does the line cue the next character's reaction? (Core word at or near end)
- Could this line be cut without losing anything? (If so, cut it)

## Practical Guidance
- Run through these questions before writing AND during revision
- If a scene feels dead, the problem is almost always at the story or scene level, not the dialogue level
- Dialogue problems are story problems — fix the design, and the words will follow
