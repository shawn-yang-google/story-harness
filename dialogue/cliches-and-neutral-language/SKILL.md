# Cliches and Neutral Language

Generic dialogue is the death of characterization. When characters speak in "neutral" language or reach for clichés, they stop being individuals and become placeholders.

## 1. The Trap of Neutral Language
Neutral language consists of generic phrases that any character could say in any situation. It lacks the "fingerprint" of a specific persona.

### Actionable Principles:
- **Find the Specificity:** Replace "I'm very angry" with a line that reflects the character's background. A dockworker, a priest, and a physicist should express anger through different metaphors and syntax.
- **Audit for Anonymity:** If you can swap a line of dialogue between two characters without changing a word, the line is likely neutral.

## 2. The Use and Abuse of Cliches
Cichés are phrases that have lost their power through overexposure. While they offer "verisimilitude" (real people use them), they drain the life out of fiction.

### Actionable Principles:
- **Identify the Expiration Date:** "Round up the usual suspects" was once brilliant; now it's a groan-shifter.
- **Subvert the Cliche:** If a character must use a cliché, let them fail to finish it, mock it, or use it ironically to signal their own lack of imagination.
- **The "Character-Specific" Fix:** Instead of a cliché, find a word or phrase so true to *this* character that it wouldn't fit anyone else.

## Key Examples:
- **Neutral:** "We need to get out of here before the police arrive."
- **Specific:** "If we're still standing on this curb when the blue lights show up, we're guests of the county for the next decade."

## Practical Guidance:
Read your dialogue aloud. If it sounds like something you've heard a thousand times on a generic TV procedural, it's time to dig deeper into the character's unique voice—their education, their region, their fears, and their specific vocabulary.
