# Empty and Emotive Talk

Dialogue loses credibility when it lacks a motivational engine or when the emotional volume is cranked up without sufficient structural support. These twin flaws destroy the reader's "bond of belief" by making characters seem like puppets rather than people.

## 1. Empty Talk: The Informational Echo
Empty talk occurs when characters tell each other what they already know (and what the audience already knows) without any underlying subtextual motivation.

### Actionable Principles:
- **Check for Redundancy:** If the line only serves to recap plot points to a character who was present for them, it's empty.
- **Find the Subtextual Why:** Every line must be an action. If a character is repeating known facts, they must be doing it to *needling*, *reassuring*, or *stalling*.
- **Omit the Obvious:** Trust the reader to remember previous scenes. Skip the "As you know, Bob" recaps.

## 2. Overly Emotive Talk: The Volume Gap
Emotive talk happens when the language used is far more intense than the actual feelings or stakes of the scene. It feels "theatrical" in the worst sense.

### Actionable Principles:
- **Match Stakes to Sentiment:** High-octane emotional outbursts (screaming, weeping, flowery declarations) require massive structural pressure to feel earned.
- **The Power of Understatement:** Often, the less a character says, the more emotion the reader projects into the silence.
- **Check the "Subtextual Bank":** If the reader looks beneath an emotional line and finds no real pain or desire, the line rings hollow.

## Key Example:
- **Empty:** "Well, here we are at the bank we planned to rob since Tuesday."
- **Emotive:** "My heart is a shattered mirror, reflecting only the void of your betrayal over this burnt toast!"

## Practical Guidance:
If a scene feels "off," strip the dialogue to its barest bones. If the scene still functions without the emotional adjectives or the recapped facts, then the original dialogue was likely empty or emotive fluff.
