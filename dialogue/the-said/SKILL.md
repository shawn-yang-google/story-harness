# The Said

The outermost sphere of dialogue content: the surface meanings that words directly express through denotations and connotations.

## Denotation and Connotation
- "Snake" literally means "legless reptile" but connotes treachery and evil
- "House" denotes domicile but carries overtones of home, hearth, family — plus undertones of shack, crash pad, flophouse
- Every word choice radiates meaning beyond its dictionary definition

## Quotable Dialogue
Lines that outlive their stories work at the level of the said:
- "Get busy livin' or get busy dyin'" (Red, THE SHAWSHANK REDEMPTION)
- "I'm just one stomach flu away from my goal weight" (Emily, THE DEVIL WEARS PRADA)

These sentences express meaning regardless of who says them or when — they operate at the surface level of universal resonance.

## Character-Specific Vocabulary
Word choices convey a character's total personality:
- **Education** or lack of it
- **Wit** or lack of it
- **Outlook on life** — optimistic, cynical, pragmatic
- **Emotional range** — stoic, volatile, measured

By creating an original style of vocabulary, diction, syntax, grammar, tone, tropes, and accent, the writer characterizes a role. These observable traits jigsaw into a personality.

## Practical Guidance
- Choose words that carry the right connotations for your character's world
- Consider: would this character say "domicile" or "pad"? "Automobile" or "ride"?
- The said creates personality — but deeper meaning lives in the unsaid and unsayable beneath it
