# Dialogue on the Page

Prose is a mental medium. It offers the widest spectrum of dialogue techniques because the reader's imagination can process internal and external speech simultaneously with more complexity than the physical senses.

## Prose as a Mental Medium
On the page, dialogue isn't just what is said; it’s how it’s framed by the Point of View (POV). The narrative voice itself becomes a form of dialogue with the reader, wrapping the characters' speech in layers of interpretation.

## POV and Dialogue Shapes
- **First Person:** The entire book is narratized dialogue. The character is telling you their story.
- **Third Person Limited:** Dialogue is filtered through one character’s biases and emotional state.
- **Omniscient:** The narrator can comment on the "truth" behind the characters' spoken lies.
- **Free Indirect Style:** The narrator’s voice adopts the tone and vocabulary of a character, blurring the line between narration and internal dialogue.

## Techniques of the Page
- **Direct Speech:** "I hate you," he said.
- **Indirect Speech:** He told her that he hated her.
- **Stream of Consciousness:** A raw, unedited flow of internal dialogue that ignores traditional grammar to capture the "feel" of thought.

## Actionable Principles
- **Use Attributions Sparingly:** Avoid "he ejaculated" or "she queried." A simple "said" is invisible; the dialogue itself should convey the emotion.
- **Master the "Beat":** Use small physical actions (beats) to break up long sections of dialogue and ground the reader in the room.
- **Vary the Mode:** Move between dramatized scenes and narratized summaries to control the story's pace.

## Practical Guidance
In prose, you have the power to show the "gap" between a character’s thoughts and their words. Use the narrative voice to highlight this tension, creating a rich, multi-layered experience that only the written word can provide.
