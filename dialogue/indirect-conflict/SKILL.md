# Indirect Conflict in Prose

Indirect conflict is a technique unique to prose. It allows a writer to layer description over dialogue to create a tension that cannot be replicated on stage or screen.

### [CLASSIC_NOVEL]: F. Scott Fitzgerald

Fitzgerald uses prose to create conflict *around* the dialogue. Characters often speak politely while the description reveals their covert antagonism.

1. **Dialogue vs. Description**
   - While the characters talk about the weather or a party, the prose describes their "tense shoulders" or the "cold light in their eyes."
   - **Actionable:** Use the narrative voice to contradict the spoken words.

2. **Covert Antagonism**
   - In high-society or repressed settings, direct conflict is forbidden. Characters must manipulate through implication and "polite" barbs.
   - **Actionable:** Have characters use compliments that are actually insults (e.g., "You're so brave to wear that").

### The Advantage of Prose

Prose can access the "inner design" of a character's thoughts *during* a conversation.
- **The Layering Technique:**
  - **Layer 1:** The spoken line (Polite).
  - **Layer 2:** The character's internal reaction (Aggressive).
  - **Layer 3:** The sensory description of the environment (Cold/Tense).

### Core Principles

- **Avoid Direct Confrontation:** The conflict should "leak" through the cracks of the conversation rather than exploding in the center.
- **The Power of the Unsaid:** In indirect conflict, what is *not* said is more important than what is.
- **Sensory Correlatives:** Use objects in the room to reflect the tension (e.g., a glass breaking, a clock ticking too loudly).

### Practical Guidance

- **The Subtext Check:** If you removed the dialogue, could the reader still feel the conflict through the description? If not, add more sensory "indirect" pressure.
- **Politeness as Mask:** Ensure your characters are "performing" for each other while the conflict simmers underneath.
