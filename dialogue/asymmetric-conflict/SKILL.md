# Asymmetric Conflict

Asymmetric conflict occurs when there is a significant power imbalance between characters. One character is usually the "aggressor" (active, verbal), while the other is the "resistor" (passive, quiet).

### A Raisin in the Sun: [SOCIAL_PLAYWRIGHT]

In Hansberry's masterpiece, we see asymmetric conflict in the way characters struggle for dignity against overwhelming social and personal odds.
- **The Aggressor:** Uses verbal action, demands, and volume to dominate space.
- **The Resistor:** Uses silence, short answers, or simple "No's" to maintain an inner fortress.

### Dynamics of Imbalance

1. **Aggressive Action vs. Quiet Resistance**
   - The tension doesn't come from an equal exchange of "blows," but from the *contrast* in styles.
   - **Actionable:** Give the powerful character long, complex lines and the "weaker" character short, punchy, or silent reactions.

2. **The Power of the "No"**
   - A character with no social power can still exercise absolute dramatic power through refusal.
   - **Actionable:** A single "No" at the end of a long, aggressive monologue can "win" the scene for the resistor.

### Core Principles

- **Contrast is Key:** The scene's energy comes from the friction between different "levels" of speech.
- **Hidden Power:** The "passive" character should still have a desire. Their "resistance" is their action.
- **Vulnerability as Weapon:** Sometimes, a character "wins" by exposing their vulnerability, forcing the aggressor to stop or reveal their own cruelty.

### Practical Guidance

- **Dialogue Length Check:** Look at the "visual" of your dialogue on the page. If it's asymmetric, one character's blocks should be significantly larger than the other's.
- **Subtextual Weight:** The character who speaks less often carries more subtextual weight. Ensure their few words are "loaded."
- **The Turn:** An asymmetric scene "turns" when the power balance shifts—even slightly—or when the resistor's silence finally breaks.
