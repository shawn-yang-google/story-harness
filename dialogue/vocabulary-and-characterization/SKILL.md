# Vocabulary and Characterization

A character's choice of words is the most direct map to their internal world. To characterize through language, we apply the "Inside-Out" method.

### The Principle of Creative Limitation

The more restricted a character's vocabulary, the more brilliant the feat of expression. Don't let every character have access to your full dictionary.
- **Principle:** Tougher technique = more brilliant feat. Limit their words to sharpen their identity.

### The Inside-Out Method

Start within the character's history, profession, and obsession, then work outward to their speech.

1. **Knowledge through Nouns and Verbs**
   - Specificity conveys mentality. A botanist doesn't see "flowers"; they see "perennials" or "Hydrangea macrophylla."
   - **Actionable:** Use specific names to signal superior knowledge or deep familiarity with a subject.

2. **[CLASSIC_FILM]lity through Modifiers**
   - Adjectives, adverbs, modals, and voice express the character's attitude and personality.
   - Does the character use "possibly" (uncertainty) or "absolutely" (arrogance)?
   - **Actionable:** Audit a character's modifiers to see if they align with their core traits.

### Key Principles

- **Diction as Mask:** Vocabulary can be used to hide or reveal. A highly educated character might use slang to fit in, or a criminal might use legalese to sound legitimate.
- **The Lexical Filter:** Filter the world through the character's unique lens. A carpenter describes a face as "badly sanded" or a relationship as "out of plumb."

### Practical Guidance

- **Noun/Verb Audit:** Highlight the nouns and verbs in a scene. If two different characters use the same specific nouns for non-common objects, their voices are overlapping.
- **Modifier Restraint:** Don't let characters over-describe unless "over-describing" is a personality flaw.
- **Inside-Out Check:** Ask: "What does this character know that no one else in the room knows?" Ensure their nouns reflect that.
