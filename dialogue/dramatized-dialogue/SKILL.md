# Dramatized Dialogue

Dramatized dialogue is the "on-the-ground" exchange between characters within a specific time and place. It is the engine of the scene, fueled by conflict and immediate tactical maneuvers.

## The Nature of the Scene
Dramatized dialogue occurs when the "fourth wall" is intact. The characters are focused on each other, unaware of the reader or audience. Every line is a reaction to the previous one, creating a chain of cause and effect that drives the tension.

## All Monologues are Dialogues
A common misconception is that a character speaking alone is giving a monologue. In drama, a character is always talking to *someone*. 
- If they are alone, they are talking to a second self, a ghost, a god, or an inanimate object.
- The "listener" provides the resistance necessary for the speech to feel active rather than expository.

## Actionable Principles
- **Lines as Volleys:** Treat dialogue like a tennis match. Each line is a return that changes the trajectory of the conversation.
- **Identify the Conflict:** Every dramatized scene requires a clash of wills. Even a simple request should meet some form of resistance.
- **Inner Dramatization:** When a character is in conflict with themselves, write it as a dramatized argument between two internal voices (e.g., the "Coward" vs. the "Hero").

## Key Examples
- **The Argument:** Two characters disagreeing on where to hide a body. Each line is a tactical push for their preferred solution.
- **The Inner Conflict:** A character looking in a mirror saying, "You're going to walk in there and tell her," followed by, "No, you're going to run."

## Practical Guidance
If a scene feels like "talking heads," increase the physical stakes or the emotional resistance. Ensure that characters are not just agreeing with each other, but are using their words to gain ground in a shifting power dynamic.
