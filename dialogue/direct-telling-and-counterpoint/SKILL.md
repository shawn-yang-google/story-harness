# Direct Telling and Counterpoint

In the struggle between "Showing" and "Telling," direct narration is often dismissed. However, "telling" has two immense virtues that "showing" lacks: Speed and Counterpoint. 

Mastery of these techniques allows a writer to bypass tedious dramatization and provide a narrative voice that ironizes or elevates the story.

## Actionable Principles

### 1. The Virtue of Speed
Dramatizing a scene takes time. A writer can spend ten pages "showing" a character's slow descent into poverty, or they can use narration to pack that exposition into a few fast, evocative words. Use "telling" to bridge gaps and maintain narrative momentum.

### 2. The Virtue of Counterpoint
The narrator's voice can contradict or ironize the themes being dramatized. If the characters are acting with false nobility, the narrator can whisper the truth of their greed. This duality creates a richer, more complex experience for the audience.

### 3. Avoiding Forced Exposition Traps
"Forced exposition" occurs when writers use artificial "in-world" methods to tell the audience things—newspapers, TV broadcasts, or characters telling each other things they both already know ("As you know, Bob..."). If you need the audience to know something, either dramatize it properly or tell them directly through narration.

## Practical Guidance

- **Audit for Speed:** If a scene feels like it's dragging just to establish a fact, try replacing it with a single paragraph of direct narration. 
- **Develop the Counterpoint:** Give your narrator a perspective. Are they sympathetic? Cynical? Unreliable? Use their voice to challenge the characters' actions.
- **Kill the 'As You Know':** Scan your dialogue for characters explaining things to each other for the audience's benefit. If found, convert it to direct narration or hide it in conflict (see *Exposition as Ammunition*).
