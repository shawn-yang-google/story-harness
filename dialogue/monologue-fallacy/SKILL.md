# Monologue Fallacy

The "Monologue Fallacy" is the belief that long, uninterrupted speeches are an effective way to convey character or plot. In reality, human interaction is a constant cycle of action and reaction.

## 1. The Speed of Speech
The average speech rate is 2-3 words per second. A 300-word speech takes 2 minutes—a massive eternity in a dramatic scene. If the other characters (and the audience) are just sitting there, the scene loses its pulse.

## 2. Internal Dialogue
Even when a character is alone, their thoughts aren't a monologue—they are an "inner dialogue" between different parts of the self (e.g., the Impulsive Self vs. the Cautious Self).

## 3. Fixes for the Long Speech:
- **Insert Nonverbal Reactions:** Show the listener flinching, yawning, or checking their watch.
- **Break into Beats:** Divide the speech into tactical shifts. Each section should have a different "action" (e.g., first attacking, then pleading, then dismissing).
- **The Impression of Length:** You can make a character *seem* long-winded without actually writing a three-page speech. Use "He went on for twenty minutes about his cat..." and then skip to the punchline.

## Key Example:
Instead of a page-long villainous monologue, have the hero try to escape, fail, insult the villain, and force the villain to react *while* they explain their plan.

## Practical Guidance:
If you have a speech longer than 50 words, look for places to cut it. If it must stay long, ensure the listener is an active participant through their silence or their physical reactions. Every word must be a "hit" that demands a response.
