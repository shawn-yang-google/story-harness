# Locution and Culture

Characters do not speak in a vacuum; they speak from the center of an absorbed culture. Locution—the style of speech—is characterization rooted in cultural icons and analogies.

### The Source of Speech

A character's culture includes everything they have absorbed: popular media, esoteric hobbies, religious upbringing, and professional jargon.

1. **Cultural Icons and Analogies**
   - Characters draw on their "icons" to make sense of the world.
   - **[CLASSICAL_PLAYWRIGHT] Example:** [ENVIED_CONSPIRATOR] compares [HISTORICAL_LEADER] to the "[ANCIENT_MONUMENT]."
   - **[CRIME_NOVELIST] Example:** Characters quote movies like "[SPY_THRILLER]" to navigate reality.

2. **The "Third Thing"**
   - Culture often provides a "Third Thing" for characters to talk about instead of their direct feelings.
   - **Actionable:** Instead of having a character say "I'm powerful," have them compare themselves to a dominant cultural figure.

### Core Principles

- **Inside the Character:** Go inside the character to see what they have seen. A character who grew up on a farm will use different analogies than one who grew up in a neon-lit city.
- **Popular vs. Esoteric:** Distinguish between characters who use mainstream cultural references ([SUPERHERO_FILM]) and those who use niche ones (17th-century French poetry). This defines their social standing and interests.

### Practical Guidance

- **Cultural Mapping:** List three cultural "touchstones" for your character (e.g., a specific sport, a brand of car, a favorite band). Use these to build their analogies.
- **Icon Usage:** When a character is under pressure, their cultural icons should emerge as a defense or an explanation.
- **Avoid Generic Talk:** If a character says "He's really big," change it to a cultural comparison: "He's the [MONSTER_FILM] of the mailroom."

### Summary
Culture is the dominant source of distinctive speech. To make a character sound real, give them a culture to draw from.
