# Dialogue Onstage

Theatre is primarily an auditory medium. On the stage, the spoken word carries the weight of the spectacle, requiring a heightened sense of rhythm, projection, and intent.

## The Auditory Primacy
In theatre, the audience relies on what they hear to build the world. This allows for conventions that would feel "fake" in film, such as the soliloquy or the aside. The stage demands language that is "larger" than life to reach the back of the house.

## Conventions of the Stage
- **Soliloquy:** A character speaking their inner thoughts aloud. It is a dramatized dialogue with the self or the audience.
- **The Aside:** A brief comment made to the audience that other characters "cannot hear."
- **Narrated Action:** Characters often describe what they are doing or seeing to enhance the audience's mental image.

## Actionable Principles
- **Write for the Ear:** Read lines aloud. Ensure they have a rhythmic flow and clear emotional beats.
- **Choose the Target:** In a soliloquy, decide if the character is talking to God, the audience, or a specific part of themselves. This changes the actor's performance.
- **Embrace Heightened Language:** Stage dialogue can afford to be more poetic or rhetorical than "naturalistic" screen dialogue.

## Practical Guidance
When writing for the stage, remember that the audience is a participant. Use the dialogue to bridge the gap between the actors and the seats, making the spoken word the primary source of action and revelation.
