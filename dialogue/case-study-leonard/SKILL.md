# Case Study: [CRIME_NOVELIST]'s Layered Dialogue

[CRIME_NOVELIST]'s *[CRIME_FILM]* features a tryst between a federal marshal ([FEDERAL_MARSHAL]) and a bank robber ([BANK_ROBBER]). It is a perfect model for how to layer dialogue to avoid being "on-the-nose."

### The Six Layers of Imagery

Leonard builds the scene through six distinct layers, ensuring the characters never speak directly about their forbidden attraction.

1. **Setting:** The snowy atmosphere of Detroit outside the hotel window.
2. **Situation:** They acknowledge they are in a "movie cliché"—the cop and the criminal in a hotel room.
3. **Escape from Reality:** They quote the movie *[SPY_THRILLER]*, using the film's lines to communicate their own situation.
4. **Memory:** They revisit their first encounter in the trunk of a car.
5. **Subtext:** The forbidden desire that stays strictly "under the ice."
6. **Dreams:** The impossible fantasy of a future together that can never happen.

### The "Third Thing" as Trialogue

Instead of a dialogue (Two-Talk), Leonard creates a **Trialogue** by introducing a "Third Thing"—in this case, the movie *[SPY_THRILLER]*.
- The characters talk *about the movie* to talk *about themselves*.
- **Actionable:** When a scene feels too direct, introduce an external cultural reference for the characters to "triangulate" through.

### Key Principles

- **Avoid the Obvious:** If they want to say "I love you," have them talk about a song or a shared memory instead.
- **Stylized Realism:** Leonard's dialogue feels real but is highly curated. Every line serves the subtext while maintaining the "mask" of the layer.
- **Double Subtext:** Both characters know the truth, and both know the other knows. This creates a vibrating tension that direct speech would kill.
