# Action vs. Activity

"Nothing is what it seems" — the primal duality of life. Activity is the surface behavior we see and hear. Action is what the character is actually doing beneath that surface.

## The Distinction
- **Activity:** Playing cards, sipping wine, chatting at a bus stop, eating ice cream
- **Action:** What you're REALLY doing — drowning sorrows, rebelling against doctor's orders, rewarding yourself for sticking to a diet

Every activity contains an action. No line of dialogue is finished until you answer: in the subtext of my character's verbal activity, what action is he in fact taking?

## Even Silence Is Action
Not speaking when a situation calls for talk is an action — perhaps a cruel one. When a character pauses, she's either reacting to the previous action or preparing her next move. "Drama" comes from Greek *draoō* = "to do" or "to act."

## The Ice Cream Test
We never eat ice cream simply because we're hungry. A conscious or subconscious action underlies the activity:
- Drowning sorrows in something sweet
- Rebelling against doctor's orders
- Rewarding himself for sticking to his diet

The same applies to talk. As Character A and B converse: Is A consoling B or ridiculing her? Is B submitting to A or dominating him? Is A feigning interest or falling in love?

## Practical Guidance
- For every scene, identify both the **activity** (what characters appear to be doing) and the **action** (what they're really doing)
- Activity is the surface manifestation; action is foundational to storytelling
- If you can't name the action beneath a character's verbal activity, the scene lacks purpose
- Test each line: what would change if I removed this dialogue? If nothing changes, the line is pure activity without action
