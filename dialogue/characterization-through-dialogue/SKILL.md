# Characterization Through Dialogue

True character is revealed by choices under pressure. Characterization is the surface — the sum of all observable traits. Dialogue is the most important trait of all.

## Three Functions of Characterization

### 1. To Intrigue
The reader/audience knows a character's appearance is not her reality. A one-of-a-kind personality makes them wonder: "That's who she seems to be, but who is she really?" This curiosity hooks involvement.

### 2. To Convince
A well-designed characterization assembles capacities and behaviors that encourage the reader/audience to believe in a fictional character as if she were factual. Coleridge's "willing suspension of disbelief" — they must accept the character's actions without doubt.

### 3. To Individualize
A well-researched characterization creates a unique combination of biology, upbringing, mentality, emotionality, education, attitudes, values, and cultural influence. The most important trait: **talk**. She speaks like no one we have ever met before.

## True Character vs. Characterization
- **Characterization** = appearance, the mask of personality
- **True Character** = reality, revealed only through risk-filled choices under pressure
- The gap between the two creates dimension and intrigue

## Key Example
[DISGRACED_SOCIALITE] (Cate Blanchett) in [COMEDIC_DIRECTOR]'s BLUE JASMINE — her language style not only sets her apart from all other cast members but from all other fictional characters.

## Practical Guidance
- Give each character a vocabulary no other character in your cast would use
- Test: cover the character name — can you identify the speaker from the words alone?
- If two characters sound interchangeable, one of them needs deeper characterization
