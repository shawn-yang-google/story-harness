# Economy and Pause

Great dialogue is the art of subtraction. By omitting the needless, you give weight to the essential. The pause is not an absence of sound, but a dam that holds back or releases emotion.

## Actionable Principles

*   **Omit Needless Words:** Follow the Strunk & White rule. If a word doesn't characterize or move the plot, cut it. Dialogue is a concentration of meaning, not a transcript of reality.
*   **The Earned Pause:** A pause must be earned. If dialogue halts repeatedly for no reason, nothing gets emphasis.
*   **Law of Diminishing Returns:** The more pauses you use, the less effective each one becomes.

## Types of Pauses

1.  **Pre-Crisis Pause:** Dams up emotion. The character is searching for words, afraid to speak, or gathering strength. It builds suspense.
2.  **Post-Turning-Point Pause:** Allows the audience (and the character) to absorb a revelation. It creates resonance.

## Key Examples

*   **[PAUSE_PLAYWRIGHT]:** Famous for the "Pinter Pause." His characters use silence as a weapon or a shield, often more expressive than their sparse, repetitive dialogue.
*   **[MINIMALIST_WRITER]:** Uses extreme economy in dialogue to show characters who are stoic, traumatized, or simply efficient. The lack of words points to the "iceberg" of subtext beneath.

## Practical Guidance

*   **The 10% Cut:** Take any scene and try to cut 10% of the words without losing the meaning. Often, you'll find the scene becomes sharper.
*   **Anchor the Pause:** Describe what is happening during the pause. Is a character staring? Fiddling with a glass? The silence must be "active."
*   **Check the Rhythm:** Read the dialogue aloud. Where do you naturally want to stop for breath or emphasis? Mark those as potential pauses.
*   **Avoid "Filler" Talk:** Cut "Hello," "How are you," and "Goodbye" unless they are loaded with subtext. Enter the scene late and leave early.
