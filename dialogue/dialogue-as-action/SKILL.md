# Dialogue as Action

Dialogue is not the imitation of conversation; it is the imitation of action through speech. Every line a character speaks must perform a specific task, aiming to change their world or the people in it.

## The Foundation Principle
Derived from the Greek *dia-legein* ("through-speech"), dialogue is the vehicle by which a story moves. Based on J.L. Austin's theory of "performatives," to say something is to *do* something. Whether a character is ordering a coffee, declaring war, or lying to themselves, they are executing an action.

## The Three Tracks of Dialogue
Characters speak on three distinct tracks, each with a different tactical goal:
1. **To Others:** Persuading, threatening, or seducing another character to gain an advantage or fulfill a desire.
2. **To Self:** Rationalizing, rehearsing, or wrestling with internal conflict. Even in silence, the mind "speaks" to itself.
3. **To Reader/Audience:** Providing context or emotional resonance through narratized modes (soliloquy or narration).

## Actionable Principles
- **Assign a Verb:** For every line, ask: "What is the character *doing* here?" (e.g., *intimidating*, *deflecting*, *pleading*).
- **Eliminate "Chitchat":** If a line only serves to pass time or mimic real-life pleasantries without a tactical goal, cut it.
- **Track the Change:** If the listener hasn't changed their mind, their mood, or their tactical position after a line is spoken, the action has failed or needs more weight.

## Key Examples
- **The Performative:** "I dub thee [LEGENDARY_KNIGHT]." The words themselves create the reality.
- **The Tactical Lie:** A character saying "I'm fine" while bleeding out is performing the action of *masking* or *protecting* others.

## Practical Guidance
When a scene feels flat, it is often because the dialogue has devolved into "talk"—words without work. Re-examine the character’s objective and force each line to be a step toward (or away from) that goal.
