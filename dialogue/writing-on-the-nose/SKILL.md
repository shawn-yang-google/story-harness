# Writing On-The-Nose

"On-the-nose" writing is when characters speak their fullest thoughts, feelings, and intentions directly. When Text = Subtext, the scene dies because there is no room for the reader to engage.

## 1. The Three Levels of Life
To avoid on-the-nose writing, remember that human interaction exists on three levels:
1. **The Said:** The actual words spoken.
2. **The Unsaid:** What is thought but kept secret.
3. **The Unsayable:** Subconscious drives the character isn't even aware of.

## 2. The Unactable Scene
A scene where two lovers stand in a room, look each other in the eye, and say "I love you" with 100% honesty is often "unactable" and boring. 
- **The Fix:** Have them talk about something else—the weather, a broken chair, a shared memory—while their *actions* and *subtext* reveal the love.

## 3. Realism vs. Nonrealism
- **Realism:** Demands subtext under virtually every line. People are defensive, polite, or deceptive.
- **Nonrealism:** Exceptions include archetypes, allegory, or certain stylized genres where "speaking the truth" is the point (e.g., Greek tragedy).

## Key Example:
- **On-the-Nose:** "I am very angry that you cheated on me, and now I am going to leave you."
- **Subtextual:** "I've packed your things. The blue suitcase had a broken zipper, so I used the trash bags. They're by the door."

## Practical Guidance:
Look for lines where a character explains their internal state. If they say "I'm sad," delete it and show them doing something that *proves* they are sad while they talk about something else entirely.
