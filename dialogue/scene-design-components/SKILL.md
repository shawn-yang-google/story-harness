# Scene Design Components

A scene is a story in miniature. It must have a beginning, middle, and end, and it must "turn" on a value. Here is the technical anatomy of a functional scene.

### 1. The Turning Point
A scene only exists if it changes a value from positive to negative (or vice versa).
- **Action:** A change in behavior or status.
- **Revelation:** A change in the character's (and audience's) knowledge.
- **Check:** At the end of the scene, is the character "better" or "worse" off than at the start?

### 2. The Beat Structure
Beats are the smallest units of action/reaction.
- **Actionable:** Label each beat with a **gerund** (e.g., *seducing, threatening, pleading, mocking*).
- **Rule:** Never use the word "saying." Gerunds force you to identify the *action* behind the dialogue, preventing "on-the-nose" writing.

### 3. The Five Steps of Behavior
Every beat follows these five steps, though they may happen in a split second:
1. **Desire:** The character wants something.
2. **Sense of Antagonism:** They realize something is in their way.
3. **Choice of Action:** They decide how to overcome the obstacle.
4. **Action:** They execute the choice (this is the dialogue/action).
5. **Expression:** The physical or verbal result.

### 4. Scene Progression
Each beat must "top" the previous one. If a character pleads and fails, the next beat shouldn't be more pleading—it should be a change in tactic (e.g., from *pleading* to *threatening*).

### Practical Guidance
- **Gerund Audit:** If you can't assign a gerund to a block of dialogue, the character isn't "doing" anything. They are just talking.
- **The Turn Check:** Compare the start of the scene to the end. If the "charge" is the same, cut the scene or find the turn.
