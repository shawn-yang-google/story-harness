# Minimal Conflict: The Power of the Unsaid

Minimal conflict relies on a delicate balance between text and subtext. It is the art of expressing profound inner tension through an economy of language, where the history between characters shadows every word.

## Actionable Principles

1.  **Prioritize Subtext**: The "text" (the spoken words) should be simple, while the "subtext" (the actual conflict) is complex and hidden.
2.  **Use Past Shadows**: Let the weight of the characters' shared history or individual pasts press down on the present moment.
3.  **Practice Economy**: Remove every word that isn't essential. Minimalist dialogue gains power from the surrounding silence.

In *[ATMOSPHERIC_DRAMA]*, Bob and Charlotte share a connection that is rarely articulated. Their conflict—against loneliness, against their marriages, against their futures—is expressed in glances, short sentences, and shared silence.

*Example*:
> BOB: I'm stuck.
> CHARLOTTE: Does it get easier?
> BOB: No. Yes. It gets easier.

The brevity of the lines masks a deep, shared existential crisis.

## Practical Guidance

*   **The "Shadow" Technique**: Before writing the scene, list the things the characters *cannot* or *will not* say to each other. These are the shadows.
*   **Focus on Action and Activity**: Since the dialogue is minimal, use activity (eating, watching TV, walking) to ground the scene and reflect the tension.
*   **Avoid Over-Explaining**: Trust the audience to feel the tension. If a character says "I'm sad," you've lost the minimal conflict. If they say "It's fine" while staring at a wall, you've found it.
*   **The Five-Word Rule**: Challenge yourself to write a pivotal emotional beat using no more than five words per character.
