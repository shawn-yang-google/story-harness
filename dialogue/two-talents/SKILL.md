# The Two Talents: Story vs. Literary

Quality dialogue is the intersection of two distinct artistic abilities: Story Talent and Literary Talent. One provides the inner architecture; the other provides the verbal finish.

### Core Principles

1. **Story Talent (Inner Design)**
   - The ability to convert raw existence into meaningful events and complex characters.
   - Dialogue failures are often actually story design failures. If the scene lacks a clear turning point or desire, no amount of "good talk" can save it.
   - **Actionable:** Before fixing a line, check if the scene's value has shifted.

2. **Literary Talent (Verbal Design)**
   - The ability to convert everyday talk into expressive, stylized dialogue.
   - This is the "voice" of the writer—the result of "genius working up a sweat."
   - **Actionable:** Read your dialogue aloud. Does it have a rhythm that transcends mere information?

### The Iceberg Technique

Borrowing from [MINIMALIST_WRITER], think of dialogue as an iceberg:
- **The Tip (Spoken Words):** Only about 10% of the character's reality is visible above the surface.
- **The Submerged Mass (Subtext):** 90% of the meaning, history, and emotion lies beneath.
- **Guideline:** If you speak the subtext, you melt the iceberg. Keep the subtext deep.

### Actionable Guidance

- **Synthesis:** Never prioritize "pretty words" over "strong actions." Story talent must lead.
- **The Sweat of Voice:** Don't look for your "voice" in a choice of font or mood. It emerges from the struggle to make the words match the inner design of the story.
- **Diagnosis:** If a scene feels flat, ask: "Is this a lack of vocabulary (Literary Talent) or a lack of conflict (Story Talent)?"

### Key Example
In a high-stakes divorce scene, a husband might ask, "Is there more coffee?"
- **Story Talent:** Designs the tension of an impending split.
- **Literary Talent:** Selects a mundane question to mask the massive subtext of abandonment.
