# Narrative Drive

Narrative drive is the force that compels the reader to ask "What happens next?" It is created when change and revelations incite curiosity, making the exposition feel like a necessary and welcome part of the journey.

## Exposition as a Side Effect
When a reader is deeply engaged in a mystery or a conflict, they *want* the facts. Exposition is no longer a "speed bump" but a "clue." The goal is to make the reader pull themselves through the telling because they need the information to understand the high stakes.

## The Revelatory Turn
Properly timed revelations create a "hook." Every time a character learns something new, it should change their tactical situation, forcing them to take a new action. This cycle of revelation and action is the heartbeat of narrative drive.

## Actionable Principles
- **Withhold for Impact:** Don't give the answer until the reader has fully felt the weight of the question.
- **Create a Trap:** Use "bureaucratic traps" or impossible situations (like in *[SATIRICAL_WAR_NOVEL]*) to force characters into repetitive, high-stakes dialogue that reveals the world's rules.
- **Connect Facts to Stakes:** If a piece of exposition doesn't raise the stakes or change the character's plan, it's not contributing to narrative drive.

## Practical Guidance
If a section of your story feels slow, check if you are providing answers to questions the reader hasn't asked yet. Increase the tension or the mystery first, then use the dialogue to provide the revelations that drive the story toward its conclusion.
