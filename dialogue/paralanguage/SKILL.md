# Paralanguage

Paralanguage is the "language alongside" the words. It encompasses gesture, body language, tone, and physical proximity. In storytelling, what is done is often more important than what is said.

## Actionable Principles

*   **Enhance or Contradict:** Paralanguage can amplify a word's meaning (a nod with "Yes") or subvert it (a nervous twitch with "I'm fine"). Contradiction creates subtext.
*   **The Performance Gap:** In scripts, actors provide the paralanguage. In prose, the writer must describe it using precise, often figurative language.
*   **Proxemics:** The physical distance between characters tells the story of their relationship. Closing the gap can be a threat or an invitation.

## Writing Nonverbal Communication

*   **Avoid Generic Beats:** "He sighed" or "She smiled" are fillers. Look for specific, character-driven actions.
*   **Sensory Description:** Use figurative language to make the gesture visceral.

## Key Examples

*   **David Means:** "He put his mouth over those words like an expensive shell." This description of how a character speaks tells us more about their hesitation and the weight of the words than the words themselves.
*   **Film Noir:** Uses shadows and lighting as a form of visual paralanguage, reflecting the character's internal moral ambiguity.

## Practical Guidance

*   **The Subtext Check:** If a scene feels flat, give a character a physical action that contradicts their words.
*   **Identify the "Tell":** Every character should have a nonverbal habit (a "tell") when they are lying, stressed, or attracted to someone.
*   **Action Beats:** Use paralanguage to break up long blocks of dialogue. It grounds the reader in the physical space and the character's body.
*   **Tone as Action:** Describe the *way* a word is spoken—the rasp, the whisper, the sudden sharpness—to convey intent without adverbs.
