# Exposition as Ammunition

Exposition is often viewed as a "dry" necessity—the dumping of information the audience needs to follow the plot. However, in masterfully written dialogue, exposition is never "dumped"; it is fired. 

When characters use what they know as weapons, information becomes tactical. It ceases to be a burden on the narrative and becomes the engine of the scene.

## Actionable Principles

### 1. The Strategy of Withholding
Information should only be revealed when its impact is maximized. If a character knows a secret, they shouldn't share it until it serves their immediate tactical goal or they are forced by extreme pressure.

### 2. Empathy + Revelation = Invisible Exposition
Exposition becomes "invisible" when the audience is emotionally invested. The moment a story-goer recognizes a shared humanity or feels deep empathy for a character, the facts delivered alongside that emotion are absorbed effortlessly.

### 3. Tactical Deployment
Ask yourself: *What does this character hope to gain by revealing this fact right now?* 
- To wound an opponent?
- To manipulate a witness?
- To protect a loved one?
- To shift the power dynamic of the room?

## Practical Guidance

- **Identify the Weapon:** Look at your scene. What piece of information does one character have that another doesn't?
- **Label the Move:** Is the character *blindsiding*, *cornering*, or *undermining* their opponent with this fact?
- **Check the Cost:** If exposition is ammunition, it should be finite. What does it cost the character to fire this shot? Does it reveal their hand? Does it burn a bridge?
