# Comic Conflict

Comedy dialogue is not just "jokes"—it is the exaggeration of balanced conflict to lunatic limits. It is driven by characters who are blind to their own absurdity.

### The [SITCOM_SERIES] Case Study: "Author, Author"

In this episode, [SITCOM_SERIES] and [SITCOM_BROTHER] attempt to write a book together. The conflict is balanced (they are brothers with equal egos) but pushed to the extreme.
- **Blind Obsession:** Both characters are fixated on their own brilliance.
- **Self-Delusion:** Neither can see that their cooperation is a disaster.

### Principles of Comic Dialogue

1. **Obsession as Engine**
   - A comic character is driven by a "super-intention" they pursue wildly unaware of the consequences.
   - **Actionable:** Give your character an obsession (e.g., "I must be the most polite person in the room") and let it collide with an obstacle.

2. **The Fixed Mind**
   - Comedy comes from a mind that cannot adapt. While dramatic characters learn and change, comic characters often double down on their errors.
   - **Actionable:** When a comic character is proven wrong, have them find a way to make it "right" within their own twisted logic.

3. **Escalation to Lunacy**
   - Take a normal conflict (e.g., who gets the last donut) and escalate it using high-status language for low-status problems.

### Practical Guidance

- **Exaggerate the Beat:** If a character is *correcting* someone in a drama, in comedy they are *pedantically deconstructing* them.
- **The Blind Spot:** Ensure the character is the only one who doesn't see why they are failing.
- **Pace and Rhythm:** Comedy dialogue often relies on "triplets" (set-up, set-up, punchline) or rapid-fire "stichomythia" (short, alternating lines).
