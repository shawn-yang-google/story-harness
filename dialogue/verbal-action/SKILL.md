# Verbal Action

Stories contain three kinds of action: mental, physical, and verbal. Dialogue is the verbal means by which characters take action in the world. What a character says suggests what she thinks; the action beneath her words expresses her identity.

## The Gerund Technique

To uncover a character's inner life, seek the subtextual action and label it with an active gerund ("-ing") phrase. This technique reveals the difference between the activity of talk and the taking of action.

### Classic Examples
- **[AMBITIOUS_THANE]** ("Tomorrow and tomorrow...") → **denouncing** existence
- **[CYNICAL_EXPAT]** ("Of all the gin joints...") → **lamenting** lost love
- **[OBSESSED_CAPTAIN]** ("Towards thee I roll...") → **blaspheming** God's power
- **[COMEDIC_PROTAGONIST]** ("Not that there's anything wrong with that") → **ridiculing** political correctness

All four express disgust, but in radically different speech styles — proving that verbal action, not vocabulary alone, defines character.

## Three Kinds of Action
1. **Mental Action:** Thoughts that cause inner change — attitude, belief, understanding
2. **Physical Action:** Gestures and tasks — body language, activities that don't require talk
3. **Verbal Action:** Words that do something — comforting, bribing, begging, refusing, attacking, defending

## Practical Guidance
- Before writing any line, ask: What does my character **want**? What **action** would he take? What **words** would carry out that action?
- Label every beat in your scene with a gerund pair: Character A is *pleading*, Character B is *dismissing*
- If you can't name the action beneath a line, the line has no purpose — cut or rewrite it
- Spoken words only move the reader/audience if the actions ring true to that specific character in that specific moment
