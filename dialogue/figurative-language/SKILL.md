# Figurative Language

Figurative language—similes, metaphors, and tropes—condenses complex emotions into sensory images. In dialogue, a well-chosen figure can do more work than a page of literal description.

## Actionable Principles

*   **The Sensory Impact Spectrum:** Metaphors lose power over time. "Lousy" is a dead metaphor (lost its connection to lice); "Sour" is still alive and carries a sensory sting.
*   **Condensation of Meaning:** Use a single image to express a mountain of subtext.
*   **Form Inspires Expression:** Let the character's background dictate their metaphors. A sailor uses the sea; a chef uses the kitchen.

## The Power of the Trope

*   **Alive vs. Dead:** Avoid clichés. If the reader doesn't *feel* the image, it's dead. Seek the "alive" metaphor that shocks the senses.
*   **Deep Effect:** The more sensory the trope, the deeper its emotional resonance.

## Key Examples

*   **[FRAGILE_ARISTOCRAT] (A Streetcar Named Desire):** "Sometimes—there's God—so quickly." These five words condense a lifetime of loneliness and the sudden, overwhelming impact of a moment of grace/hope.
*   **[NOIR_NOVELIST]:** Famous for similes that define a world (e.g., "The minutes dragged by like a wounded soldier"). They characterize the narrator's cynical, weary perspective.

## Practical Guidance

*   **Check for Clichés:** If a character says "it's raining cats and dogs," they aren't being figurative; they're being lazy. Find a unique image.
*   **Match the Stakes:** Don't use a cosmic metaphor for a minor inconvenience. Reserve heightened figurative language for turning points.
*   **Simplicity is Strength:** Often the most powerful metaphors are the simplest. "He was a wall" tells us more about a character's stubbornness than a paragraph of adjectives.
*   **The "Sensory" Test:** Ask: can I see, smell, taste, or feel this metaphor? If the answer is no, it's an abstract comparison, not a trope.
