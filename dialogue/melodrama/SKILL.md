# Melodrama

Melodrama is frequently misunderstood as "over-acting" or "purple prose." In reality, it is a structural failure: it occurs when the intensity of a character's expression exceeds the motivation provided by the stakes.

## 1. Expression vs. Motivation
Dialogue becomes melodramatic when a character screams over a triviality or delivers a high-flown monologue for a low-stakes conflict.

### Actionable Principles:
- **Scale the Volume:** Ensure the emotional intensity of the dialogue is proportional to what is at risk. 
- **The Understatement Rule:** High power and high stakes often demand quieter, more controlled dialogue. A king who whispers "Crucify him" is more terrifying than one who screams it.
- **Check the Motivation:** If a character is being "extra," ask: what is driving this? If the answer is "nothing much," you have melodrama.

## 2. The Internal Balance
Melodrama is the result of under-motivation. To fix it, you don't necessarily change the words; you increase the stakes or deepen the character's internal pressure.

### Actionable Principles:
- **Build the Pressure Cooker:** Before a character "breaks" emotionally, show the reader the forces preventing them from breaking. 
- **Action > Adjectives:** Let the character's desperate choices prove their emotion, rather than relying on superlative language.

## Key Example:
- **Melodramatic:** "I shall never forgive you for forgetting to call! My soul is a withered husk in the desert of your neglect!" (Over-expression for a minor slight).
- **Dramatized:** "I waited by the phone until 3 AM. Then I threw it in the river." (Understatement + Action).

## Practical Guidance:
If a scene feels "too much," don't just reach for a thesaurus to find simpler words. Look at the scene's architecture. Is the conflict strong enough to support the character's reaction? If not, either lower the reaction or raise the stakes.
