# Misshapen Scenes

A scene is a unit of story where something *changes*. If the scene ends and the "values" (safety vs. danger, love vs. hate) are exactly where they started, the scene is misshapen—or rather, it's a "nonevent."

## 1. Turning Point Timing
The turning point is the moment where the scene's direction shifts irrevocably.
- **Too Soon:** The climax happens at the start, and the rest of the scene is an anticlimactic slide.
- **Too Late:** The audience sees the ending coming from a mile away, and the characters take too long to catch up.
- **Absent:** The characters talk but nothing happens. No one gains or loses ground.

## 2. Splintered Scenes
This is a disconnect between a character's **inner intentions** (what they want) and their **outer behaviors** (what they say and do).
- **The Fix:** Ensure the dialogue is a *tactic* used to achieve the inner intention, even if the tactic is indirect (subtext).

## 3. The Paraphrasing Trap
Writers often try to fix a "boring" scene by rewriting the words (paraphrasing). Usually, the problem isn't the words—it's the **story design**. 
- **The Fix:** If the scene is dead, don't change the dialogue. Change the *stakes*, the *motivation*, or the *turning point*.

## Practical Guidance:
For every scene, identify:
1. **Starting Value:** (e.g., Character A is confident).
2. **The Turning Point:** (e.g., Character B reveals a secret).
3. **Ending Value:** (e.g., Character A is humiliated).
If you can't find these three components, the scene needs a structural redesign, not a line-edit.
