# Listen

Developing an ear for dialogue is the writer's most fundamental practice. It cannot be taught in a formula — it must be cultivated through disciplined listening, reading, and rewriting.

## Listen on Two Levels
1. **Surface Level:** What people actually say — their word choices, rhythms, verbal tics, pauses, interruptions
2. **Action Level:** What people are DOING beneath their words — persuading, deflecting, seducing, dominating, pleading

Train yourself to hear both simultaneously. In every conversation you overhear, ask: what is the real negotiation happening beneath these pleasantries?

## Build Vocabulary
- Read voraciously across genres, periods, and cultures
- The wider your vocabulary, the more precise your character voices can become
- Pay attention to how different authors handle dialogue — their rhythms, their economies, their excesses

## Read the Good, Rewrite the Bad
- **Read the good** to internalize what works — let great dialogue teach your unconscious ear
- **Rewrite the bad** to practice — take flat dialogue from mediocre work and improve it as an exercise
- This dual practice builds both taste and technique

## Record and Play Back
- Record your own dialogue aloud after every draft
- When you hear it performed (even by yourself), accidental echoes, dead phrases, and false notes pop out instantly
- What reads well on the page may sound terrible spoken — and vice versa

## Practical Guidance
- Eavesdrop deliberately — buses, cafés, waiting rooms are free workshops
- Never copy what you hear word-for-word; real conversation is repetitive and flat
- Instead, listen for the ACTION beneath the talk, then express that action in character-specific language
- Your ear develops over years, not days — be patient and persistent
