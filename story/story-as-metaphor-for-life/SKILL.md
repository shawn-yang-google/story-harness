# Story as Metaphor for Life

A storyteller is a **life poet** — an artist who transforms day-to-day living into a poem whose rhyme scheme is **events rather than words**.

## The Balance
A story must abstract from life to discover its essences, but **not become so abstract** that it loses all sense of life-as-lived. It must be like life, but not so verbatim that it has no depth beyond what's obvious.

## Two Types of Failure
### The "[CLASSIC_FILM]l Story" (Understructured)
- Slice-of-life portraiture mistaking **verisimilitude for truth**
- The writer believes that more precise observation of daily facts = more truth
- But **fact is truth with a small "t"** — Big "T" Truth is behind, beyond, inside the surface

### The "Guaranteed Commercial Success" (Overstructured)
- Overcomplicated assault on the senses mistaking **kinesis for entertainment**
- Uses story as excuse for CGI spectacle
- Like amusement park rides — pleasures are short-lived

## The Core Truth
> What happens is **fact**, not truth. Truth is **what we think about** what happens.

**Facts are neutral.** The weakest excuse to include anything: "But it actually happened." Joan of Arc's facts are always the same, but each writer finds different "truth" in her life.

**Abstractions are neutral.** Visual effects, editing rhythms, color saturation have no meaning in themselves — they must serve story.
